From contextual conditions to entrepreneurial sustainability through entrepreneurial resilience: A multi-method analysis of women diaspora entrepreneurs

Abstract
Purpose - Women diaspora entrepreneurs navigate intersecting gender, migration, and institutional barriers by orchestrating transnational resources into sustainable ventures; yet integrated theoretical and empirical models of these pathways remain largely untested. Drawing on opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory and transnational embeddedness perspective, this study examines how social, cultural, digital, and institutional conditions drive Strategic Opportunity Activation (SOA), and how SOA subsequently contributes to Entrepreneurial Sustainability (ES) through the mediating role of Entrepreneurial Resilience (ER).
Design/Methodology/Approach - A quantitative cross-sectional survey design was employed, collecting data from 697 women diaspora entrepreneurs operating small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in the Delhi-NCR region. Data were analyzed using a hybrid PLS-SEM and fsQCA approach, enabling simultaneous examination of symmetric net effects and asymmetric configurational pathways.
Findings - Family support, bicultural competence, digital competence, digital autonomy, transnational social capital, cultural authenticity, host country inclusiveness, and policy and ecosystem support each significantly enhance strategic opportunity activation. SOA emerges as the most powerful predictor of entrepreneurial sustainability, confirming its central role in converting contextual resources into long-term economic, social, and environmental value. Entrepreneurial resilience significantly mediates the SOA sustainability relationship, indicating that resilience amplifies the sustainability payoff of effective opportunity activation. The fsQCA reveals six equifinal configurational pathways to entrepreneurial sustainability, underscoring the substitutability of digital, cultural, and social resources.
Originality/Value - This study makes four novel contributions: (1) it introduces and validates Strategic Opportunity Activation as a theoretically grounded action-mechanism linking contextual embeddedness to sustainability; (2) it repositions entrepreneurial resilience as a process-level mediator rather than a dispositional trait; (3) it employs a methodologically pluralistic PLS-SEM–fsQCA design; and (4) it advances context-sensitive entrepreneurship theory by centering the experiences of women diaspora entrepreneurs in an emerging economy setting.
Keywords: Strategic opportunity activation; Entrepreneurial sustainability; Entrepreneurial resilience; Women diaspora entrepreneurs; Digital competence; Transnational social capital
Paper Type: Research paper

1. Introduction
The global entrepreneurial landscape has been fundamentally reshaped by accelerating international mobility, digital disruption, and the rise of sustainability as a central performance imperative. With over 281 million international migrants worldwide (representing 3.6% of the global population) diaspora communities have become increasingly significant actors in contemporary entrepreneurial ecosystems (United Nations, 2020). Diaspora entrepreneurship, defined as the creation and management of ventures by individuals who maintain meaningful transnational ties to both home and host country contexts, generates economic value, stimulates innovation, and facilitates cross-border knowledge transfer at a scale that conventional domestic entrepreneurship cannot replicate (Portes et al., 2002; Drori et al., 2009; Brzozowski et al., 2017). Within this broader phenomenon, women diaspora entrepreneurs constitute a particularly dynamic yet empirically underserved cohort. Women represent a significant and growing proportion of the global migrant population, and a growing body of evidence confirms that women-led diaspora ventures make distinctive contributions to economic development, social capital formation, and cross-cultural market creation (Brieger & Gielnik, 2021; Vershinina et al., 2019; Kabbara & Zucchella, 2023). In dynamic host environments such as India’s National Capital Region (one of South Asia’s most densely networked migration hubs) women from diaspora backgrounds increasingly leverage transnational resources, cultural identities, and digital platforms to bridge multiple institutional and market contexts (Talukdar, 2024; Steel, 2021).
Central to the long-term viability of diaspora ventures is the concept of Entrepreneurial Sustainability (ES) understood not merely as venture survival but as the dynamic capacity to generate economic value, maintain social legitimacy, and adapt to environmental change through the continuous integration of economic, social, and ecological considerations (Shepherd & Patzelt, 2011; Muñoz & Cohen, 2018; Lazarte-Aguirre, 2024). In complex entrepreneurial settings characterized by dual institutional embeddedness, intersecting identity pressures, and resource constraints, the pathways to entrepreneurial sustainability remain poorly theorized and empirically fragmented. Existing models predominantly conceptualize sustainability as a function of resource availability (emphasizing access to social capital, financial resources, or institutional support) without adequately theorizing the strategic mechanisms through which these resources are actively converted into sustainable outcomes (Lahiri et al., 2025; Sarma et al., 2024). This resource-centric perspective fails to capture the agency-intensive, iterative processes through which women diaspora entrepreneurs navigate uncertainty, activate latent opportunities, and construct sustainable ventures across multiple cultural and institutional environments (McMullen & Shepherd, 2006; Alvarez & Barney, 2007).
A substantial body of literature has advanced understanding of diaspora entrepreneurship across several dimensions. The mixed embeddedness framework established that immigrant entrepreneurial outcomes are co-determined by the intersection of individual resources and the structural opportunity spaces created by host country markets and institutions (Kloosterman & Rath, 2001). Building on this, transnational entrepreneurship research has demonstrated how diaspora entrepreneurs leverage bicultural knowledge, cross-border social capital, and institutional bridging capabilities to identify and exploit market asymmetries (Portes et al., 2002; Drori et al., 2009; Elo & Dana, 2019). Research on social embeddedness has confirmed the centrality of family support and community networks in enabling opportunity recognition, resource mobilization, and risk management (Arregle et al., 2015; Levy et al., 2021). Digital entrepreneurship scholarship has shown how platforms and digital competencies can partially compensate for geographic constraints and institutional barriers (Nambisan, 2017; Kraus et al., 2019; Steel, 2021). Simultaneously, sustainable entrepreneurship theory has evolved toward a process-oriented view that foregrounds entrepreneurial action as the critical link between embedded resources and long-term value creation (Dean & McMullen, 2007; Schaltegger et al., 2019; Shepherd & Patzelt, 2011).
Notwithstanding these advances, significant theoretical, methodological, contextual, geographical, and empirical gaps persist. Conceptually, existing models lack an integrative, action-oriented mechanism linking the diversity of contextual conditions facing women diaspora entrepreneurs to sustainability outcomes. Opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory posits that sustainability depends not on resource possession per se but on the entrepreneur’s deliberate capacity to identify, enact, and exploit opportunities (Shane & Venkataraman, 2000; McMullen & Shepherd, 2006); yet this mechanism remains untested in gendered diaspora contexts. Theoretically, while entrepreneurial resilience has been recognized as a critical capability in adverse environments (Bullough & Renko, 2013; Shepherd et al., 2019) its mediating role in the SOA–sustainability relationship has neither been theorized nor empirically examined. Methodologically, prior research has predominantly employed qualitative case studies or symmetric regression models, missing the configurational complexity inherent in diaspora entrepreneurship (Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021; Pappas & Woodside, 2021). Contextually, the gendered dimensions of diaspora entrepreneurship about how women strategically leverage cultural authenticity, negotiate institutional inclusiveness, and exercise digital autonomy, remain undertheorised (Vershinina et al., 2019; Mousa et al., 2024). Geographically, the dominant focus on Western European and North American contexts leaves South Asian diaspora ecosystems largely unexplored, echoing Welter’s (2011) call for more context-sensitive entrepreneurship research.
To address these gaps, this study introduces Strategic Opportunity Activation (SOA) as a theoretically grounded, action-oriented construct: the deliberate, iterative process through which entrepreneurs recognize, evaluate, mobilize, and enact opportunities embedded within their contextual environments. Drawing on opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory (Shane & Venkataraman, 2000; Alvarez & Barney, 2007; McMullen & Shepherd, 2006) and transnational embeddedness perspective (Kloosterman & Rath, 2001; Bagwell, 2018), SOA is positioned as the strategic bridge between contextual resource endowments and sustainable outcomes. This conceptualization is further grounded in resource orchestration theory (Sirmon et al., 2011), which argues that competitive advantage emerges not from resource possession alone but from the entrepreneur’s capacity to structure, bundle, and leverage resource portfolios into value-creating action. The study also integrates Entrepreneurial Resilience (ER) as a mediating mechanism, building on evidence that resilience (the capacity to persist, adapt, and recover from adversity) significantly amplifies the effectiveness of opportunity-oriented behavior in sustaining entrepreneurial performance (Bullough & Renko, 2013; Fisher et al., 2014; Shatila et al., 2025).
The significance of this inquiry is multidimensional. Theoretically, it advances sustainable entrepreneurship scholarship by situating it within a transnational and gendered framework, operationalising Welter’s (2011) contextualization agenda in diaspora settings. It also enriches resilience theory by repositioning ER from a static personal trait to a dynamic process-level mediator. Methodologically, the hybrid PLS-SEM and fsQCA design responds to calls for pluralistic research methods that can simultaneously capture net effects and configurational complexity (Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021). Practically, the findings generate actionable insights for policymakers and ecosystem architects seeking to design interventions that enhance opportunity activation capabilities and resilience among women diaspora entrepreneurs, thereby fostering sustainable entrepreneurial outcomes. Three interrelated research questions guide this study:
RQ1: How do social, cultural, digital, and institutional conditions influence strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs?
RQ2: Does entrepreneurial resilience mediate the relationship between strategic opportunity activation and entrepreneurial sustainability?
RQ3: What configurations of antecedent conditions are sufficient to achieve entrepreneurial sustainability among women diaspora entrepreneurs?
This study makes four primary theoretical contributions. First, it introduces and empirically validates SOA as an action-oriented construct that integrates contextual embeddedness with strategic behavior in diaspora women’s entrepreneurship. Second, it demonstrates entrepreneurial resilience as a process-level mediator rather than merely a personal disposition, advancing resilience theory in the entrepreneurship domain. Third, the hybrid PLS-SEM–fsQCA methodology captures both linear relationships and equifinal configurational pathways, offering methodological pluralism that single-method studies cannot provide. Fourth, by centering women diaspora entrepreneurs in an emerging economy context, this study responds to persistent calls for context-sensitive, gender-aware, and geographically inclusive entrepreneurship research (Welter, 2011; Brieger & Gielnik, 2021; Vershinina et al., 2019).
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature and develops the hypotheses. Section 3 presents the theoretical framework. Section 4 describes the research methodology. Section 5 reports the analysis and results. Section 6 discusses the findings in relation to existing theory and evidence. Section 7 outlines theoretical and managerial implications. Section 8 presents the conclusion, limitations, and future research directions.

2. Review of related literature and hypothesis development
2.1 Social conditions
2.1.1 Transnational social capital
Transnational social capital (TSC) refers to resources embedded within cross-border social networks that can be mobilized for entrepreneurial purposes, including access to information, finance, legitimacy, and emotional support (Levy et al., 2021; Menzies et al., 2003; Levy et al., 2013). Structural holes theory suggests that networks spanning disconnected social clusters generate non-redundant information flows that confer entrepreneurial advantages in opportunity identification and exploitation (Burt, 2004). For women diaspora entrepreneurs, who frequently encounter structural barriers such as constrained access to formal finance, gendered labour markets, and institutional limitations in host countries, TSC functions as a primary compensatory mechanism that substitutes for formal institutional support (Brieger & Gielnik, 2021; Mustafa & Chen, 2010). Prior research establishes the centrality of social capital in immigrant opportunity creation: transnational networks facilitate access to insider knowledge of home and host country markets, enabling the identification of cross-border arbitrage opportunities that remain invisible to locally embedded entrepreneurs (Levy et al., 2021; Portes et al., 2002). Arregle et al. (2015) confirm that strong social ties enhance venture growth by supporting resource mobilization and strategic coordination, though excessive embeddedness may constrain strategic flexibility. Through social ties, diaspora entrepreneurs access market intelligence, build trust-based relationships, and leverage community legitimacy that transforms opportunity identification into actionable entrepreneurial initiatives (Evansluong & Ramírez-Pasillas, 2019). From a resource orchestration perspective, TSC constitutes a bundle of relational resources that entrepreneurs activate to structure and leverage opportunity-enabling configurations (Sirmon et al., 2011). Accordingly, we propose:
H1. Transnational social capital positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.
2.1.2 Family support
Family support represents the instrumental, emotional, and informational assistance provided by family members that enables entrepreneurial venture creation and growth (Edelman et al., 2016; Osorio et al., 2017). From a transnational perspective, diaspora networks enable entrepreneurs to operate across borders by leveraging family and community ties for bootstrapping, recruitment, and market entry (Pruthi & Wright, 2017). Karayianni et al. (2023) demonstrate that “Family-across-borders” capital enhances entrepreneurial preparedness through the transfer of norms and cross-border resources, a mechanism particularly salient for women entrepreneurs managing dual domestic and professional roles. Aldrich and Cliff’s (2003) family embeddedness perspective establishes that family networks exert pervasive effects on entrepreneurial resource mobilization, opportunity recognition, and strategic positioning, effects that are amplified in diaspora contexts where formal institutional support may be limited. Jamaludin et al. (2020) further show that family support and geographical proximity jointly shape opportunity recognition, with transnational family ties compensating for physical distance through trust and shared norms. Brambini-Pedersen et al. (2025) find that immigrant entrepreneurs strategically bridge indigenous, immigrant, and transnational networks, with women often leveraging family-based trust while cultivating weak ties for growth opportunities. Resource orchestration theory reinforces that family-based relational assets function as foundational inputs that entrepreneurs bundle into opportunity-enabling resource configurations (Sirmon et al., 2011; Powell & Eddleston, 2013). Thus:
H2. Family support positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.

2.2 Cultural conditions
2.2.1 Cultural authenticity
Cultural authenticity denotes the intentional preservation, performance, and strategic expression of cultural heritage (including values, narratives, aesthetics, language, rituals, and practices) within entrepreneurial activities (Altinay et al., 2026). Women diaspora entrepreneurs frequently embed authentic cultural elements into their offerings, particularly in sectors where symbolism and experiential meaning strongly influence consumer evaluation, such as food, fashion, wellness, and digitally mediated cultural services (Mousa et al., 2024). By integrating cultural narratives and practices into products and services, entrepreneurs identify and exploit niche demands across ethnic, transnational, and hybrid markets that are inaccessible to culturally undifferentiated competitors. From a gendered perspective, cultural authenticity acquires heightened strategic significance as women often face intersecting constraints related to gender, ethnicity, and migrant status (Altinay et al., 2026; Kouam, 2025). Ketheswaran (2025) demonstrates that women in post-conflict and fragile institutional environments strategically leverage cultural identity as a source of entrepreneurial credibility, trustworthiness, and legitimacy, thereby countering marginalization and repositioning themselves within host-country entrepreneurial ecosystems. Effective opportunity activation therefore depends on the entrepreneur’s capacity to curate, reinterpret, and adapt authentic cultural elements in alignment with evolving market conditions while preserving cultural meaning (Mousa et al., 2024; Elo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Accordingly:
H3. Cultural authenticity positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.
2.2.2 Bicultural competence
Bicultural competence refers to the capacity to navigate, integrate, and flexibly switch between home and host country cultural systems, encompassing cognitive flexibility, cultural empathy, communication proficiency, and behavioural adaptability (Morris et al., 2015; Hong et al., 2000). Entrepreneurs with high bicultural competence are better positioned to interpret diverse institutional logics, consumer preferences, and business norms, allowing them to recombine knowledge across cultural contexts and identify cross-cultural opportunities that remain invisible to monoculturally embedded entrepreneurs (Lakshman, 2013). Prior research establishes that women diaspora entrepreneurs often function as cultural intermediaries who bridge markets, facilitate knowledge transfer, and activate opportunities emerging at the intersection of multiple cultural systems (Xu et al., 2019). Bicultural competence enhances negotiation effectiveness, alliance formation, and legitimacy building in host country environments, thereby mitigating the liability of foreignness and supporting proactive opportunity activation (Mousa et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Carrera and Wei (2014) confirm that bicultural individuals demonstrate higher adaptive capacity and cognitive flexibility, capabilities that translate directly into entrepreneurial responsiveness to cross-cultural market signals. When combined with cultural authenticity, bicultural competence enables women diaspora entrepreneurs to balance cultural preservation with strategic adaptation, facilitating sustainable growth through contextually grounded opportunity activation (Ketheswaran, 2025). Thus:
H4. Bicultural competence positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.

2.3 Institutional conditions
2.3.1 Host country inclusiveness
Host country inclusiveness denotes the extent to which the institutional, regulatory, and sociocultural environment of a destination country accommodates diverse entrepreneurial actors, particularly women from diaspora backgrounds, and thereby shapes their capacity to recognize and act upon strategic opportunities (Marcinkowski et al., 2025). Welter (2011) argues compellingly that entrepreneurial action is inherently shaped by its social, institutional, and spatial contexts, and that context-insensitive theoretical account produce incomplete and potentially misleading explanations of entrepreneurial outcomes. Inclusiveness in both formal and informal institutions is critical for mitigating the structural constraints traditionally encountered by underrepresented entrepreneurs positioned at the intersection of gender and migrant status (Boudreaux & Nikolaev, 2018; Rahmonov, 2025). Ecosystems characterized by inclusive policies, recognition of foreign qualifications, and equitable access to financial and social resources are more likely to facilitate opportunity-driven entrepreneurship among diaspora women, narrowing persistent gender gaps in strategic opportunity activation (Ketheswaran, 2025; Boudreaux & Nikolaev, 2018). Conversely, exclusionary institutions and discriminatory practices intensify the liabilities of foreignness and gender, constraining diaspora women’s entrepreneurial entry and strategic positioning (Rakhmonov, 2025). Host country inclusiveness thus functions as a critical contextual condition that either enables or constrains SOA by shaping both the external opportunity landscape and the perceived legitimacy of entrepreneurial endeavours. Accordingly:
H5. Host country inclusiveness positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.
2.3.2 Policy and ecosystem support
Gender-responsive entrepreneurship policies (including access to finance, tax incentives, capacity building initiatives, and incubation programs) play a pivotal role in reducing structural and institutional barriers that constrain women and migrant entrepreneurs (Kim & Jin, 2024). For women diaspora entrepreneurs, these interventions are particularly salient given their exposure to intersecting challenges related to gender norms, migration status, limited institutional embeddedness, and restricted access to mainstream business networks. Autio et al. (2014) and Stam (2015) demonstrate that well-designed entrepreneurial ecosystems generate externalities that enable the emergence and scaling of diverse ventures through coordinated institutional, financial, and knowledge-based support. Inclusive policy-driven ecosystems enhance the performance and sustainability of women- and minority-owned enterprises by facilitating access to capital, mentorship, markets, and professional networks essential for opportunity recognition and exploitation (Matenda et al., 2024). Empirical evidence confirms that ecosystem actors (including government agencies, financial institutions, non-profit organizations, incubators, and diaspora associations) collectively strengthen women’s entrepreneurial agency and capability development through coordinated support mechanisms (Brambini-Pedersen et al., 2025). These mechanisms provide the institutional infrastructure necessary for SOA while integrating women diaspora entrepreneurs into the broader economic fabric of the host country. Thus:
H6. Policy and ecosystem support positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.

2.4 Digital conditions
2.4.1 Digital autonomy
The accelerating digitalization of economic life has profoundly transformed entrepreneurial processes with particularly significant implications for women diaspora entrepreneurs whose ventures operate across national, cultural, and institutional boundaries (Steel, 2021). Digital autonomy (the degree to which individuals can independently access, control, and strategically deploy digital technologies to pursue personal and economic objectives) has emerged as a critical enabler of strategic opportunity activation (Kim & Jin, 2024). Studies demonstrate that among women entrepreneurs, digital autonomy enhances decision-making freedom, reduces reliance on traditional gatekeepers, and facilitates opportunity creation beyond localized markets. Nambisan (2017) argues that digital technologies reshape the very nature of entrepreneurial action by enabling continuous opportunity enactment across distributed platforms and markets. In fragile and post-conflict settings, where gendered constraints limit entrepreneurial agency, digital autonomy partially compensates for weak physical ecosystems by enabling remote market access and virtual network formation (Ketheswaran, 2025). However, autonomy remains contingent on intersectional factors, including migration status, gender norms, and unequal access to digital infrastructure (Badiuzzaman et al., 2025; Pei & Chib, 2021). Lyngstadaas and Berg (2024) and Chong and Zainal (2024) further confirm that digital empowerment contributes to enhanced decision-making agility and entrepreneurial performance among individuals in digitally demanding work environments. Therefore:
H7. Digital autonomy positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.
2.4.2 Digital competence
Digital competence refers to the set of skills, knowledge, and capabilities required to critically and creatively using digital technologies for economic and entrepreneurial purposes (Yu & Moon, 2021). Digital technologies function as catalysts for entrepreneurial transformation only when women possess the competence to appropriate them meaningfully (Pei & Chib, 2021). For diaspora entrepreneurs, digital competence facilitates opportunity recognition through market intelligence, online learning, and integration into global value chains that transcend geographic and institutional constraints. Badiuzzaman et al. (2025) show that migrant communities with higher levels of digital adaptation are better positioned to exploit entrepreneurial opportunities despite institutional and policy gaps in host countries. From a resource-based perspective, Moustafa et al. (2025) contend that in restrictive environments, women entrepreneurs rely on conditional resource accumulation, with digital competence acting as a key personal resource that buffers contextual constraints. Kim and Jin (2024) confirm that digital capabilities exert a significant positive effect on entrepreneurial performance among SMEs, while Bjola and Manor (2022) caution that digital competence alone does not guarantee empowerment, strategic intentionality is required for opportunity activation. The interaction between digital competence and autonomy creates a synergistic capability that enables diaspora entrepreneurs to navigate global digital markets and exploit cross-border opportunities effectively (Nambisan, 2017; Kraus et al., 2019). Accordingly:
H8. Digital competence positively influences strategic opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs.

2.5 Strategic opportunity activation, entrepreneurial resilience, and entrepreneurial sustainability: Mediation analysis
The extant literature increasingly conceptualizes strategic opportunity activation as a dynamic, iterative process involving the identification, evaluation, mobilization, and exploitation of opportunities under conditions of uncertainty (Zahra et al., 2014; McMullen & Shepherd, 2006). Alvarez and Barney (2007) distinguish between opportunity discovery and opportunity creation, both of which require deliberate strategic activation to generate value, a distinction that is especially pertinent in diaspora contexts where opportunities often must be actively constructed from the intersection of home and host country market asymmetries. From a resource orchestration perspective, SOA represents the bundling and leveraging phase of the resource management process: the point at which contextual resources are combined into strategic configurations and deployed to create competitive advantages (Sirmon et al., 2011). Within diaspora contexts, this process is inherently transnational, embedded in multiple institutional environments, and shaped by cross-border social capital and bicultural knowledge (Elo & Dana, 2019; Brzozowski et al., 2017).
However, the conversion of identified opportunities into sustainable outcomes is frequently impeded by structural constraints, liability of foreignness, identity tensions, and institutional volatility. The literature increasingly emphasizes entrepreneurial resilience as the critical capability enabling entrepreneurs to sustain opportunity-oriented action in turbulent environments (Shepherd et al., 2019; Bullough & Renko, 2013). Entrepreneurial resilience refers to the capacity to persist, adapt, and recover from adversity while maintaining proactive and opportunity-focused behaviour (Fisher et al., 2014; Ayala & Manzano, 2014). Prior research on sustainable entrepreneurship suggests that long-term sustainability depends on the entrepreneur’s ability to continuously activate opportunities while balancing economic viability, social embeddedness, and adaptive learning (Schaltegger et al., 2019; Shepherd & Patzelt, 2011). Building on this, recent studies theorize and empirically demonstrate the mediating role of entrepreneurial resilience, showing that resilience strengthens the SOA–sustainability relationship by enabling resource reconfiguration, innovation, and persistence amid stress and uncertainty (Shatila et al., 2025; Alshebami & Murad, 2022; Nasser, 2021). Evidence from migrant entrepreneurship further indicates that high resilience amplifies the positive effects of opportunity activation on venture continuity and social impact (Wu et al., 2023; Williams et al., 2017; Kipkosgei, 2022). Conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 2001) provides further theoretical grounding, proposing that resource-rich individuals initiate positive gain spirals wherein strategic actions generate resource gains that reinforce adaptive capacity and sustained performance. Accordingly:
H9. Strategic opportunity activation positively influences entrepreneurial sustainability.
H10. Strategic opportunity activation positively influences entrepreneurial resilience.
H11. Entrepreneurial resilience positively influences entrepreneurial sustainability.
H12. Entrepreneurial resilience mediates the relationship between strategic opportunity activation and entrepreneurial sustainability.

3. Theoretical framework
The present study is theoretically grounded in an integrative framework that synthesizes opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory and the transnational embeddedness perspective, embedded within the broader contextual turn in entrepreneurship research (Welter, 2011). This dual theoretical synthesis directly addresses the causal complexities inherent in migrant-owned ventures, where entrepreneurial opportunities emerge not from isolated individual traits but from configured embeddedness across home–host country contexts. The integrative framework is illustrated in Figure 1.
Opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory posits that entrepreneurial outcomes are shaped not merely by the identification of opportunities but fundamentally by the entrepreneur’s capacity to act upon and transform those opportunities into value-creating ventures (Shane & Venkataraman, 2000; McMullen & Shepherd, 2006). Shane and Venkataraman (2000) establish that entrepreneurial opportunities are neither uniformly distributed nor uniformly recognized; access depends on prior knowledge, information asymmetries, and the entrepreneur’s embeddedness in opportunity-rich networks. Alvarez and Barney (2007) extend this by distinguishing discovery and creation as complementary pathways through which opportunities are activated. This theory is particularly relevant for diaspora entrepreneurs, whose opportunities emerge from cross-border market asymmetries and cultural knowledge differentials (Arejiogbe et al., 2023; Sheriff & Muffatto, 2015). However, these opportunities are subject to high uncertainty, legitimacy challenges, and institutional complexity, meaning that sustainability depends less on opportunity recognition per se and more on the entrepreneur’s capacity to strategically activate opportunities under complex, multi-layered conditions. Thus, SOA is theorized as the central mechanism linking contextual conditions to entrepreneurial sustainability.
The transnational embeddedness perspective complements this by positing that entrepreneurial actions are inherently shaped by the social, cultural, and institutional contexts in which entrepreneurs are situated across both home and host countries (Abd Hamid et al., 2019; Bagwell, 2018; Rusinovic, 2008). This dual embeddedness provides access to diverse resources (transnational social capital, family support, and cultural knowledge) while simultaneously exposing entrepreneurs to institutional complexities, competing identity demands, and regulatory heterogeneity. Welter’s (2011) contextualization framework reinforces that entrepreneurship is a deeply situated phenomenon in which the characteristics of the social, institutional, and temporal context fundamentally shape which opportunities are perceived, which resources are mobilized, and which sustainability pathways are available. Applied to the current study, this perspective explains why social, cultural, digital, and institutional conditions exert differential effects on SOA depending on the gendered and transnational position of the entrepreneur.
This integrated framework advances entrepreneurship theory by demonstrating that social, cultural, institutional, and digital conditions do not directly determine entrepreneurial sustainability; rather, they operate through their influence on the entrepreneur’s capacity to strategically activate opportunities within transnational contexts. Entrepreneurial resilience is theorized as a mediating boundary condition, the mechanism through which the sustainability payoff of SOA is amplified or attenuated. Resilient entrepreneurs are better equipped to sustain strategic initiatives, recalibrate under uncertainty, and remain committed to long-term sustainability goals despite institutional setbacks, cultural role strain, and competitive pressures (Williams et al., 2017; Shatila et al., 2025). From a conservation of resources perspective (Hobfoll, 2001), SOA initiates positive resource gain spirals: the accumulation of opportunity-based resources (knowledge, networks, confidence) builds resilience, which in turn reinforces the sustainability of entrepreneurial performance. Collectively, this mediation framework positions SOA as the pivot between contextual embeddedness and sustainable outcomes, with resilience functioning as the amplifying mechanism that translates strategic activation into durable value creation.

4. Research methodology
4.1 Research design
This study employs a quantitative, cross-sectional, explanatory research design using a survey-based approach, which is well-suited for examining hypothesized relationships among latent constructs and extending theory in entrepreneurship research (Hair et al., 2019). Cross-sectional designs are widely employed in entrepreneurship studies that aim to establish structural relationships among conceptually distinct constructs at a single point in time (Hair et al., 2019), and are appropriate for the current study’s objective of testing an integrative path model within a well-defined entrepreneurial cohort. To address the study’s focus on both net effects and causal complexity, a dual analytical strategy combining Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling (PLS-SEM) and Fuzzy-Set Qualitative Comparative Analysis (fsQCA) was employed. This hybrid approach has been validated as a methodologically superior design for capturing the asymmetric and configurational nature of entrepreneurial phenomena that conventional symmetric methods alone cannot reveal (Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021; Pappas & Woodside, 2021). PLS-SEM examines the hypothesized linear relationships and mediating effects within the proposed model and fsQCA complements this analysis by uncovering equifinal configurational pathways through which different combinations of conditions produce high entrepreneurial sustainability.

4.2 Sample and data collection
The target population comprised women diaspora entrepreneurs operating small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) in the Delhi-NCR region. A non-probability purposive sampling strategy was employed to identify respondents meeting the specific characteristics of the study population (Etikan et al., 2016). Participants from the diaspora entrepreneurial community were recruited through business associations, diaspora networks, incubators, women’s entrepreneurship forums, and online entrepreneurial platforms to ensure contextual relevance. Snowball sampling facilitated access to this otherwise difficult-to-reach population. Participation was voluntary, and all respondents were informed of the academic purpose of the study prior to participation. Following the 10-times rule and formal statistical power analysis for PLS-SEM (Hair et al., 2019), an initial sample of 725 responses was collected through offline survey administration. After screening for incomplete or low-quality responses, a final analytical sample of 697 respondents was retained, substantially exceeding minimum requirements and thereby enhancing statistical power, representativeness, and structural model robustness (Hair et al., 2019).

4.3 Measures
All constructs were operationalised using multi-item reflective scales adapted from established prior literature to ensure content validity, conceptual alignment, and psychometric adequacy. Data were collected using a structured questionnaire on a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree to 5 = Strongly Agree). A pilot study (n = 50) was conducted prior to full-scale data collection to assess item clarity, scale reliability, and face validity; items were refined and reorganized based on pilot feedback. 4.4 Statistical methods
4.4.1 PLS-SEM (Symmetric approach)
PLS-SEM was conducted in two stages. The first stage assessed the measurement model to confirm internal consistency, reliability, and validity of all reflective constructs (Hair et al., 2019). Internal consistency was evaluated using Cronbach’s alpha and composite reliability (ρa and ρc), with threshold values of 0.70 and 0.70 respectively. Convergent validity was assessed through Average Variance Extracted (AVE), with the minimum criterion set at 0.50, indicating that each construct accounts for the majority of variance in its indicators. Discriminant validity was evaluated using the Fornell–Larcker criterion and Heterotrait-Monotrait (HTMT) ratios, with an HTMT threshold of 0.90. The second stage tested the hypothesized structural relationships using a bootstrapping procedure (n = 5,000 subsamples; 95% confidence interval). Predictive model fit was evaluated using R², f², Q², SRMR, and NFI indices.
4.4.2 fsQCA (Asymmetric approach)
Following PLS-SEM, fsQCA was employed to examine asymmetric, non-linear, and configurational effects of antecedent conditions on entrepreneurial sustainability (Pappas & Woodside, 2021; Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021). FsQCA analysis proceeded in three stages. First, raw scores were calibrated into fuzzy membership scores (ranging from 0 = non-membership to 1 = full membership) using 95th, 50th, and 5th percentile values as calibration anchors (Ragin, 2009). Second, a necessary condition analysis was performed using a consistency threshold of 0.90 to identify any condition whose presence was consistently necessary for the outcome to occur (Vis & Dul, 2018). Third, a truth table was constructed using a frequency cut-off of 1 and a consistency threshold of 0.95, and intermediate solutions were derived for analytical interpretation (Fiss, 2011; Kallmuenzer et al., 2019; Chuah et al., 2021). The combination of PLS-SEM and fsQCA enables a richer, pluralistic understanding of entrepreneurial sustainability by simultaneously identifying the most important individual predictors (PLS-SEM) and the sufficient configurational combinations that produce the outcome (fsQCA) (Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021).

5.3.1 Mediation analysis
The mediation analysis confirms that entrepreneurial resilience significantly mediates the SOA–sustainability relationship. The direct path from SOA to ES is positive and statistically significant (β = 0.169, p < 0.001), confirming that opportunity-driven strategies directly enhance sustainable outcomes. SOA also exerts an exceptionally strong effect on ER (β = 0.708, p < 0.001), indicating that active opportunity engagement materially strengthens adaptive capacity. In turn, ER exerts a substantial positive effect on ES (β = 0.659, p < 0.001). The significant indirect effect (β = 0.467, p < 0.001) confirms partial mediation, with resilience transmitting approximately 73% of the total indirect effect of SOA on sustainability. These findings are consistent with conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 2001), suggesting that SOA initiates positive resource gain spirals that accumulate resilience, which subsequently reinforces sustainable entrepreneurial performance.

The six configurations highlight several theoretically meaningful pathways to entrepreneurial sustainability. 6. Discussion
The combined PLS-SEM and fsQCA findings illuminate how women diaspora entrepreneurs in Delhi-NCR transform social, digital, cultural, and institutional resources into sustainable ventures through the strategic mechanism of SOA, amplified by entrepreneurial resilience. The results advance several strands of the entrepreneurship and sustainability literature in theoretically meaningful ways.
Family support and transnational social capital emerge as the most influential social predictors of SOA. The strong effect of TSC is consistent with structural holes theory (Burt, 2004), which argues that networks bridging disconnected clusters generate non-redundant information advantages that enhance opportunity identification. In diaspora contexts, TSC provides access to insider knowledge of both home and host country markets, enabling the identification of transnational arbitrage opportunities that remain invisible to locally embedded competitors (Levy et al., 2021; Portes et al., 2002). The comparable effect of family support reinforces social embeddedness theory (Aldrich & Cliff, 2003), demonstrating that proximal, affect-laden ties provide emotional reassurance, informal financing, and risk buffering that lower the threshold for opportunity activation under uncertainty, effects that are especially pronounced for women navigating dual domestic and entrepreneurial roles (Powell & Eddleston, 2013; Mustafa & Chen, 2010). Collectively, these findings affirm that opportunity activation is not a solitary cognitive act but a socially enabled process rooted in dense relational contexts.
Digital competence and digital autonomy demonstrate strong and comparable effects on SOA, affirming the centrality of digital capabilities in contemporary diaspora entrepreneurship. Consistent with digital entrepreneurship theory (Nambisan, 2017; Kraus et al., 2019), entrepreneurs possessing advanced digital skills and deployment autonomy are better positioned to experiment with business models, access global markets, and rapidly recombine resources in volatile environments. These results reinforce resource orchestration theory (Sirmon et al., 2011): digital resources function not merely as operational tools but as strategic assets that entrepreneur structure and bundle into opportunity-enabling configurations. The finding that digital autonomy operates largely independently of institutional support (as evidenced in Configuration 4 of the fsQCA) is particularly notable, suggesting that digitally empowered diaspora entrepreneurs may partially compensate for institutional exclusivity through self-directed digital market engagement (Steel, 2021; Pei & Chib, 2021).
Cultural authenticity and bicultural competence exert significant and theoretically meaningful effects on SOA. Cultural authenticity enables women diaspora entrepreneurs to differentiate offerings in niche markets, build trust-based legitimacy across ethnic and hybrid consumer segments, and reframe cultural identity as a strategic resource rather than a source of disadvantage (Ketheswaran, 2025; Kouam, 2025). Bicultural competence extends this advantage by enabling cognitive flexibility, cross-cultural communication, and institutional bridging that facilitate opportunity activation across multiple cultural and business environments (Lakshman, 2013; Xu et al., 2019). These results support mixed embeddedness theory (Kloosterman & Rath, 2001) and the transnational embeddedness perspective (Bagwell, 2018), confirming that cultural resources embedded within diaspora identity are not passive heritage markers but active strategic instruments in the opportunity activation process.
Host country inclusiveness and policy ecosystem support demonstrate positive but comparatively moderate effects on SOA, suggesting that institutional conditions function as enabling factors in opportunity activation. This finding is consistent with Welter’s (2011) contextual perspective, which posits that institutions shape the opportunity landscape but do not substitute for individual and relational agency. The fsQCA corroborates this interpretation: institutional conditions appear in only one of six configurations (Configuration 6), underscoring their supporting rather than necessary role. This result aligns with Boudreaux and Nikolaev (2018), who demonstrate that formal institutional quality is a facilitating, rather than sufficient, condition for opportunity-driven entrepreneurship. Importantly, the finding that policy ecosystem support exerts the weakest effect on SOA among the eight antecedents suggests a gap between formal policy intentions and their practical impact on diaspora women’s entrepreneurial agency, a finding with direct implications for policy design (Matenda et al., 2024).
The results provide robust empirical support for the centrality of SOA as the primary mechanism converting contextual resources into entrepreneurial sustainability. The strong direct effect of SOA on ES confirms that entrepreneurs who actively identify, enact, and reconfigure opportunities are better positioned to achieve economic viability, social legitimacy, and long-term adaptability. This is entirely consistent with opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory: sustainability depends not on static resource endowments but on the continuous orchestration and activation of opportunities in response to environmental change (Shane & Venkataraman, 2000; McMullen & Shepherd, 2006; Shepherd et al., 2013). The exceptionally strong effect of SOA on ER (β = 0.708) further establishes that repeated engagement with uncertainty substantially builds adaptive capacity, confidence, and resilience orientation.
The mediation analysis provides particularly compelling theoretical insight. The significant indirect effect of SOA on ES through ER (β = 0.467) confirms partial mediation and is consistent with conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 2001), which proposes that resource-rich individuals initiate positive gain spirals: SOA generates resource gains (knowledge, networks, legitimacy) that strengthen resilience, which in turn reinforces sustainable performance. This “resilience amplification” mechanism has important theoretical implications: it suggests that opportunity activation does not directly translate into sustainability at full efficiency, but resilience is the critical transmitter that ensures activated opportunities generate durable, long-term value. Findings from the fsQCA reinforce this: entrepreneurial resilience appears in all six sufficient configurations as a core condition, confirming its near-necessary status and its role as the indispensable thread linking opportunity activation to sustainability across diverse contextual configurations. This is aligned with Williams et al.’s (2017) conceptualization of resilience as a dynamic organizational response that integrates adaptive learning with forward-oriented strategic commitment.
The fsQCA findings reveal a theoretically rich pattern of causal complexity and equifinality. No single configuration dominates; rather, six distinct pathways (combining social, cultural, digital, and institutional conditions in different proportions) all produce entrepreneurial sustainability when ER and SOA are present. This pattern has two important implications. First, it confirms that digital and sociocultural resources are functionally substitutable: strong family and cultural embeddedness (Configurations 2 and 3) can produce sustainability outcomes in the absence of digital competence, while digital autonomy and competence (Configurations 1 and 5) can drive sustainability even without strong institutional backing. This substitution effect is consistent with resourcefulness theory in entrepreneurship (Baker & Nelson, 2005), which argues that entrepreneurs in resource-constrained environments creatively recombine available resources to achieve desired outcomes. Second, the consistent presence of SOA across all six configurations confirms its status as a quasi-necessary condition for sustainability, reinforcing its theoretical centrality in the proposed model.

7. Implications of the study
7.1 Theoretical contributions
This study makes several substantial theoretical contributions to entrepreneurship and sustainability literature. First, the introduction and empirical validation of Strategic Opportunity Activation as a theoretically grounded, action-oriented construct advances sustainable entrepreneurship theory by repositioning the explanation of sustainability from a resource-possession perspective to a resource-activation perspective. This shift from “what resources do entrepreneurs have” to “how do entrepreneurs strategically activate resources into opportunities” responds directly to calls for more dynamic, process-oriented accounts of entrepreneurial sustainability (Lahiri et al., 2025; Sarma et al., 2024). SOA operationalises the “strategic enactment” dimension of opportunity-based entrepreneurship theory (Shane & Venkataraman, 2000; Alvarez & Barney, 2007) in a novel empirical domain where it had not previously been tested.
Second, the study enriches resilience theory in entrepreneurship by repositioning ER as a dynamic, process-level mediating mechanism rather than a static personal trait or dispositional resource. By demonstrating that ER mediates the SOA–sustainability relationship, the study shows that resilience does not act independently of strategic behavior; rather, it is cultivated through and amplifies the impact of opportunity activation. This contributes to the growing literature on the dynamic nature of entrepreneurial resilience (Shatila et al., 2025; Bullough & Renko, 2013) and extends conservation of resources theory (Hobfoll, 2001) to the domain of diaspora women’s sustainable entrepreneurship.
Third, the study advances the contextual turn in entrepreneurship research (Welter, 2011) by demonstrating how gendered, transnational, and digitally mediated contexts shape the pathways from contextual conditions to sustainability. The integration of social, cultural, digital, and institutional dimensions within a single unified model offers a more complete, multi-layered account of diaspora women’s entrepreneurship than prior single-dimension studies. By situating the analysis within an emerging economy (Delhi-NCR), the study also contributes to geographical diversification of the entrepreneurship evidence base (Brieger & Gielnik, 2021; Vershinina et al., 2019).
Fourth, the hybrid PLS-SEM–fsQCA methodology contributes methodologically by demonstrating how symmetric and asymmetric analytical approaches can be meaningfully combined to generate plural insights. The PLS-SEM results identify net effects while the fsQCA uncovers equifinality, causal asymmetry, and the substitutability of conditions, together providing a richer theoretical account than either method alone (Rasoolimanesh et al., 2021). The revelation that no single condition is uniformly necessary (except ER) challenges monolithic models that prioritize single pathways to entrepreneurial success.

7.2 Managerial implications
The prominence of family support and digital capabilities in driving SOA has clear implications for practitioners and support organizations. Incubators, accelerators, and diaspora entrepreneurship programs should prioritize the development of digital competence and autonomy alongside relational capital, recognizing these as complementary and mutually reinforcing enablers of opportunity activation. Training programs that strengthen digital marketing, e-commerce, virtual networking, and platform-based market intelligence would directly enhance diaspora women’s capacity to identify and exploit transnational opportunities. At the same time, mentorship models that leverage family-embedded and community-based trust, particularly for first-generation diaspora women that would strengthen the relational scaffolding on which SOA depends.
The mediating role of entrepreneurial resilience has direct implications for capacity building. Entrepreneurship development programs should integrate resilience-building components (including stress management, adaptive learning, failure-recovery frameworks, and cognitive reappraisal techniques) alongside technical business skills training. Organizations should also create peer support networks and facilitated communities of practice in which diaspora women entrepreneurs can share experiences of adversity, collectively build resilience repertoires, and sustain strategic commitment over time. The fsQCA finding that ER is present in all configurations reinforces that resilience investment yields sustainability benefits regardless of the entrepreneur’s specific resource profile or strategic pathway.

7.3 Policy implications
The comparatively weaker effects of host country inclusiveness and policy ecosystem support on SOA suggest a substantive gap between formal policy intentions and practical outcomes for women diaspora entrepreneurs. Policymakers should undertake systematic reviews of the accessibility, reach, and gender-responsiveness of existing entrepreneurship support programs, ensuring that diaspora women are explicitly included in program design, eligibility criteria, and outreach strategies. Specific recommendations include: (1) establishing dedicated diaspora women’s entrepreneurship desks within national and regional incubation systems; (2) developing multilingual, culturally sensitive business development programs that recognize bicultural competencies and transnational market knowledge as entrepreneurial assets; (3) creating policy incentives for digital infrastructure access and digital skills development in diaspora communities; and (4) simplifying regulatory processes for migrant-owned SMEs, including faster recognition of foreign qualifications and streamlined business registration procedures. The fsQCA’s Configuration 6 demonstrates that when host country inclusiveness combines with rich sociocultural embeddedness and resilience, it generates a comprehensively supportive pathway to sustainability, underscoring the multiplier effect of aligned institutional and relational support.

8. Conclusion, limitations, and future research directions
8.1 Conclusion
This study investigated how social, cultural, digital, and institutional contextual conditions drive strategic opportunity activation, and how SOA subsequently contributes to entrepreneurial sustainability through the mediating mechanism of entrepreneurial resilience among women diaspora entrepreneurs in Delhi-NCR. Employing a hybrid PLS-SEM and fsQCA research design on a sample of 697 women diaspora entrepreneurs, the study generated several theoretically significant and practically relevant findings.
Across all eight antecedents, transnational social capital, family support, digital competence, and digital autonomy emerged as the most influential drivers of SOA, confirming that effective opportunity activation among women diaspora entrepreneurs requires the simultaneous cultivation of relational, digital, cultural, and institutional resources. SOA, in turn, proved to be the central mechanism connecting these contextual conditions to sustainable entrepreneurial outcomes, exerting both a significant direct effect on entrepreneurial sustainability and an exceptionally strong effect on entrepreneurial resilience. The mediation analysis confirmed that entrepreneurial resilience amplifies the sustainability payoff of strategic opportunity activation, transmitting approximately three-quarters of SOA’s total effect on sustainability through an adaptive gain-spiral mechanism. The fsQCA revealed six equifinal configurational pathways to sustainability, confirming that different combinations of social, cultural, and digital resources can each constitute sufficient routes to the same sustainability outcome, provided that both SOA and ER are present.
Together, these findings reframe the theoretical account of diaspora women’s entrepreneurial sustainability from a static resource-endowment explanation to a dynamic, action-oriented framework in which strategic agency (operationalised as SOA) and adaptive capacity (operationalised as ER) serve as the twin engines of long-term entrepreneurial performance. The study contributes an empirically validated, theoretically grounded, and practically actionable model of diaspora women’s entrepreneurial sustainability that advances both scholarly understanding and policy effectiveness.

8.2 Limitations and future research directions
Several limitations of this study merit acknowledgment and suggest productive avenues for future research. First, the cross-sectional survey design captures relationships at a single point in time, precluding causal inference or longitudinal tracking of how SOA and resilience evolve over the entrepreneurial lifecycle. Future studies should employ longitudinal or experience-sampling designs to capture the dynamic, iterative nature of opportunity activation and resilience development over time.
Second, the study is geographically limited to women diaspora entrepreneurs in Delhi-NCR, an emerging economy context with distinctive institutional and cultural characteristics. While this focus provides valuable context-specificity, it limits the generalizability of findings to diaspora communities in developed economies, different host-country institutional environments, or other geographic regions. Comparative multi-country studies that examine whether the identified pathways hold across different host country contexts would substantially advance theoretical generalizability (Brieger & Gielnik, 2021).
Third, the study focuses exclusively on women diaspora entrepreneurs. While this gender-specific focus enables deep theoretical insight into gendered entrepreneurial dynamics, it limits comparative analysis with male diaspora entrepreneurs or mixed-gender cohorts. Future research should adopt gender-comparative designs that examine whether SOA mechanisms, resilience processes, and sustainability pathways differ systematically between women and men within diaspora entrepreneurial ecosystems.
Fourth, common method bias remains a potential concern in self-reported, single-source survey studies. Although procedural remedies (Harman’s single factor test, full collinearity VIF assessment) were applied and yielded no evidence of significant bias, future studies should employ multi-source data collection, including objective venture performance indicators, to corroborate self-reported sustainability measures.
Fifth, the study does not examine sectoral heterogeneity. The drivers and configurations of SOA and sustainability may differ substantially between knowledge-intensive sectors (technology, finance, consulting) and culturally embedded sectors (food, fashion, wellness, arts). Sector-stratified analyses would enrich understanding of how industry context shapes entrepreneurial pathways among diaspora women.
Finally, future research should theoretically and empirically incorporate the role of artificial intelligence, platform ecosystems, and emerging digital technologies as new contextual conditions shaping opportunity activation and resilience among diaspora entrepreneurs. As digital transformation continues to reshape entrepreneurial ecosystems globally, understanding how diaspora women leverage AI-enabled tools and digital platforms to activate cross-border opportunities represents a particularly timely and policy-relevant research agenda.
