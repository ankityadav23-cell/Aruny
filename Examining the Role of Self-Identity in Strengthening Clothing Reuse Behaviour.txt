Identity-driven sustainable consumption: Examining the role of self-identity in strengthening clothing reuse behaviour

Abstract
This study advances the discourse on sustainable consumption by examining how self-identity shapes clothing reuse behaviour within an identity-driven framework of circular fashion. Grounded in identity and consumption theory, the research investigates the multidimensional drivers of reuse intention and their contribution to environmental sustainability outcomes. Specifically, the study aims to assess the influence of skill-based empowerment on behavioural intention to reuse clothing, while also evaluating the moderating role of sustainability-oriented self-identity. A quantitative research design was employed using survey data collected from 531 respondents in the Delhi NCR region, and the hypothesized relationships were tested using structural modeling techniques. The findings reveal that tailoring skills and resources, replacement avoidance, hedonic motives, nostalgic motives, and emotional attachment significantly strengthen reuse intention, which in turn positively predicts environmental sustainability outcomes. Emotional and competence based factors exert stronger effects than purely economic considerations, underscoring the symbolic and experiential dimensions of reuse behaviour. Importantly, self-identity demonstrates a dual role: it directly reinforces environmental sustainability orientation but negatively moderates the intention–behaviour relationship, suggesting potential intention–behaviour decoupling driven by moral licensing and identity saturation effects. The study contributes theoretically by integrating competence, emotion, and identity perspectives in sustainable fashion research and offers managerial and policy implications emphasizing skill development, emotional durability design, and identity-based communication strategies to foster scalable clothing reuse practices.

Keywords Circular Fashion, Clothing Reuse, Environmental Sustainability, Self-Identity, Sustainable Consumption

1. Introduction
The environmental consequences of contemporary clothing consumption are increasingly alarming. Accelerated production cycles, fast fashion business models, shortened garment lifespan, and escalating textile waste have positioned the fashion industry among the most resource-intensive sectors globally (Centobelli et al., 2022). In response, sustainability scholarship has emphasized strategies that slow material throughput by extending product lifecycles, particularly through reuse, repair, resale, and recirculation practices (Niinimäki, 2013; Niinimäki & Hassi, 2011). Clothing reuse is defined as the continued utilization of garments through repeated wearing, repairing, altering, sharing, or repurposing has emerged as a critical mechanism for reducing replacement demand and mitigating environmental impact (Fletcher, 2016).
Despite broad recognition of reuse as environmentally beneficial, dominant models of sustainable consumption continue to conceptualize pro-environmental behaviour is largely driven by intention. Frameworks such as the theory of planned behaviour (TPB) and the value–belief–norm theory posit that environmental concern, moral obligation, and behavioural intentions are the principal drivers of sustainable action (Ajzen, 2020; Stern, 2000). Within this tradition, clothing reuse is typically interpreted as a conscious, environmentally motivated choice undertaken by individuals seeking to reduce their ecological footprint. Although these approaches have generated valuable insights into sustainable attitudes and intentions (Truelove et al., 2014), they also reveal a persistent attitude-behaviour gap, wherein positive environmental values do not consistently translate into everyday sustainable practices (Kollmuss & Agyeman, 2002).
Emerging research suggests that sustainable consumption behaviours may also be shaped by self-identity processes rather than solely by explicit environmental intentions. Identity theory posits that individuals enact behaviours that align with salient self-concepts, such as being “responsible,” “thrifty,” “caring,” or “resourceful” (Burke & Stets, 2009). In consumption contexts, identity-driven actions may generate sustainability outcomes even when ecological motivations are secondary or implicit (Oyserman & Destin, 2010). From a social practice perspective, everyday behaviours are sustained through the interplay of materials, competences, and meanings embedded in routine life (Shove et al., 2012; Reckwitz, 2002). Thus, clothing reuse may persist not because individuals consciously aim to act sustainably, however such practices align with their self-identity and socially reinforced meanings of care, durability, and prudence.
Indian region provides a particularly instructive context for examining identity-driven reuse behaviour. The country sustains dense informal clothing ecosystems, including neighbourhood tailors (darzis), household sewing skills, and culturally embedded norms of garment maintenance and alteration (Breman, 1996). Clothing reuse practices, such as repairing, resizing, repurposing, or passing garments within families are common across socioeconomic groups. Importantly, these behaviours are often motivated by identity related meanings of responsibility, attachment, and value consciousness rather than explicit environmental concern (Pong & Tam, 2023). Even among upwardly mobile consumers, reuse practices frequently persist, suggesting that self-identity and habit may stabilize sustainable behaviours across income trajectories (McCollough, 2006).
However, existing sustainability research has rarely integrated self-identity into empirical models of clothing reuse. Much scholarship privileges environmental attitudes over identity alignment, and often relies on self-reported intentions rather than behavioural indicators such as garment longevity or reduced replacement frequency (Kollmuss & Agyeman, 2002). Consequently, the mechanisms through which identity strengthens reuse behaviour remain underexplored. Addressing this gap, the present study conceptualizes clothing reuse as an identity-driven form of sustainable consumption. Focusing on Indian adults aged 25 to 50 years, the research examines how self-identity, particularly identities associated with responsibility, thrift, and care that strengthens clothing reuse behaviours and contributes to measurable sustainability outcomes, including extended garment lifespan and reduced purchasing frequency. By integrating identity theory with social practice perspectives, this study advances sustainable consumption scholarship beyond intention centric frameworks and highlights the central role of self-identity in normalizing and stabilizing environmentally beneficial consumption practices.

2. Review of related literature and hypothesis development
2.1 Tailoring skills and resources
From a network perspective reuse practices are embedded within interconnected systems of actors, infrastructures, and competencies, where individual skills such as repair, alteration, and creative redesign interact with social and institutional support to sustain reuse behaviours (Ekström & Salomonson, 2014). Laitala & Boks (2012) further contend that sustainable clothing consumption depends not only on product design but also on user’s competencies, highlighting that skills in maintenance, repair, and modification significantly extend garment lifetimes. Empirical evidence by Zhang & Hale (2022) demonstrates that lack of repair skills, limited access to tailoring services, and insufficient knowledge act as barriers, whereas availability of community repair initiatives and learning opportunities serve as key enablers of repair and repurpose practices. Similarly, Gray et al. (2022) show that alternative business models, such as rental, resale, and repair services provide structural resources that facilitate prolonged clothing use and reduce environmental impact. Wu et al. (2022) extend this argument by emphasizing community based reuse and upcycling services as social innovation mechanisms that enhance consumer capability and motivation to engage in circular fashion behaviours. Collectively, these studies suggest that tailoring skills (individual capability) and supportive resources (structural enablers) jointly strengthen consumer’s behavioural intention to reuse clothing by lowering perceived barriers and increasing perceived control and value. Therefore, it is proposed that:
H1. Tailoring skills and resources have a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.2 Replacement Avoidance
Replacement avoidance, defined as consumer’s deliberate efforts to delay or prevent the purchase of new garments by extending the functional life of existing clothing, has emerged as a critical behavioural strategy for promoting sustainable fashion consumption (Maguire & Fahy, 2023). Prior research highlights that extending clothing lifetimes through alternative business models such as repair services, resale, and sharing platforms can substantially reduce the environmental impact associated with textile production and disposal (Gray et al., 2022). Similarly, Zhang & Hale (2022) emphasize that repair and repurposing behaviours are influenced by both structural enablers (e.g., access to repair services, skills, and supportive policies) and psychological factors such as environmental awareness and perceived responsibility. McNeill et al. (2020) further demonstrate that consumer’s intentions toward garment life extension practices are shaped by product related factors including perceived damage, brand attachment, and replacement cost, suggesting that higher perceived value and emotional attachment can discourage premature disposal. Complementing these findings, Diddi & Yan (2019) identifies attitudes toward repair, perceived behavioural control, and consumption barriers as significant predictors of repair oriented behaviours, reinforcing the role of pro-environmental attitudes and practical feasibility in replacement avoidance decisions. Collectively, the literature suggests that replacement avoidance is not merely an economic decision but a value driven and context dependent behaviour that may positively influence consumer’s behavioural intention to reuse clothing. Accordingly, it is proposed that:
H2. Replacement avoidance has a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.3 Hedonic Motives
The practice of repairing and mending clothing is increasingly driven by hedonic motivations that prioritize emotional pleasure, creativity, and experiential meaning over purely economic or environmental considerations (Kemi & Zilahy, 2025). Consumers find intrinsic enjoyment in creative self expression through visible mending and customization, develop emotional attachment and nostalgia toward personally meaningful garments, and value the uniqueness and authenticity of repaired apparel as an alternative to standardized mass fashion (Bye & McKinney, 2007). Engagement in clothing repair also resonates with the slow fashion movement, fostering feelings of care, responsibility, and personal fulfilment while reinforcing identity and values aligned with sustainability (Niinimäki et al., 2020). The hedonic appeal of clothing repair thus lies in the joy of restoration, the storytelling embedded within garments, and the emotionally rewarding, self affirming experience of prolonging a product’s life, transforming repair from a functional act into a meaningful consumption experience (Halicki et al., 2024; Gwilt, 2020). Accordingly, it is proposed that:
H3. Hedonic motives have a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.4 Nostalgic Motives
Using repaired clothing is strongly shaped by nostalgic, emotional, and psychological motivations, functioning as a form of “wearable memory” that connects individuals to their personal histories, family ties, and earlier life stages (Niinimäki & Armstrong, 2013). Beyond practicality, mended garments embody “wearable nostalgia,” allowing people to preserve sentimental items, such as inherited jackets or childhood sweaters that symbolize comfort, continuity, and identity. Repaired clothes often act as mnemonic artefacts, anchoring memories of specific people, places, and experiences, while visible mending practices (e.g., sashiko or boro) celebrate imperfection and aging, aligning with wabi-sabi aesthetics and authentic storytelling (De Castro, 2021). These practices also reflect intergenerational transmission of skills and values, reinforcing cultural heritage and familial bonds. Moreover, the revival of repair resonates with a nostalgic “make do and mend” ethos, expressing a longing for slower, more intentional consumption and serving as a subtle resistance to fast fashion’s disposability (Rogers, 2021). Through mending, individuals reclaim personal history, reconnect with earlier selves, and assert durable, emotionally meaningful relationships with clothing (Gwilt, 2020; Niinimäki et al., 2020). Accordingly, it is proposed that:
H4. Nostalgic motives have a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.5 Emotional Attachment
Emotional attachment to clothing constitutes a central psychological mechanism underpinning sustainable garment care, as it significantly enhances consumer’s intentions to repair, maintain, and retain apparel rather than prematurely discard it (Zhou et al., 2026). Conceptualized as emotional durability, this attachment develops through memory associations that connect garments to meaningful life events, identity congruence whereby clothing reflects personal values and self-concept, and psychological ownership fostered through personalization, mending, and prolonged use (Park, 2025; Niinimäki et al., 2020). Empirical evidence consistently demonstrates that emotionally valued garments are more likely to be repaired, with consumers willing to invest time, skills, and financial resources, while the act of repair itself further reinforces attachment through effort justification and anticipated positive emotions, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of sustainable clothing behaviour (Diddi & Yan, 2019; McNeill & Moore, 2015). Such emotionally durable wardrobes are characterized by stewardship oriented use practices, routine care, and extended product lifespan that may span years or even decades, often embedded within cultural norms and community based repair practices (Aliabieva et al., 2025; Potdar et al., 2024). However, the translation of attachment into sustained repair behaviour is contingent upon contextual enablers, including supportive social norms, focused engagement during DIY repair, and access to affordable repair services as well as constraints, wherein garments are cognitively devalued despite remaining functionally viable (Özen et al., 2025; Zhang & Hale, 2022). Accordingly, it is proposed that:
H5. Emotional attachment has a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.6 Economic Value
Repairing clothes delivers substantial economic value by extending garment lifespan, displacing the need for new purchases, often by a large margin and supporting a circular economy that lowers waste disposal and production related costs (Diddi & Yan, 2019). By enabling repairs such as patching, altering, or darning, consumers maximize the utility and return on their initial clothing investments, reduce long term consumption expenditure, and are encouraged to purchase higher quality, durable garments that offer superior lifetime value compared to fast fashion alternatives (Iran et al., 2024). At the same time, clothing repair enhances resource efficiency by reducing demand for new production, thereby lowering environmental and municipal waste management costs, while also stimulating local economies through increased demand for tailoring, mending, and repair related services (Karuppiah, 2026). Collectively, repair and upcycling practices transform worn garments into valuable, functional products, weaken reliance on disposable fashion, and promote circular business models in resale and restoration, reinforcing a more sustainable and value driven consumption system (Potdar et al., 2024; Zhang & Hale, 2022). Accordingly, it is proposed that:
H6. Economic value has a positive effect on behavioural intention to reuse.

2.7 Behaviour Intention to Reuse, Self-Identity and Environmental Sustainability: Moderation analysis
Self-identity serves as a significant psychological and moral determinant of sustainable consumption through repaired clothing, shaping both direct and interactive effects on environmental sustainability outcomes. Drawing on identity theory and symbolic consumption perspectives, individuals who perceive themselves as environmentally responsible, frugal, or anti-consumerist are more likely to adopt garment repair practices as an expression of their core values and moral commitments (Tajfel & Turner, 2004; Belk, 1988). Such identity congruent behaviours transform clothing repair from a utilitarian necessity into a value driven lifestyle choice that challenges the dominant “take–make–dispose” paradigm of fast fashion (Niinimäki, 2013). Consistent with the TPB (Ajzen, 1991), behavioural intention to reuse is expected to positively influence environmental sustainability  as stronger intentions increase the likelihood of engaging in repair and reuse practices that extend product lifecycles and reduce textile waste (Niinimäki et al., 2020). Furthermore, self-identity is posited to exert a direct positive effect on environmental sustainability, given that individuals with a strong pro-environmental self-concept are more inclined toward consistent and responsible consumption behaviours (Van der Werff et al., 2013; Whitmarsh & O’Neill, 2010). Importantly, self-identity is also proposed to moderate the relationship between behavioural intention to reuse and environmental sustainability, such that the intention–sustainability link becomes stronger when reuse behaviours align with one’s self-concept. In this regard, self-identity functions as a psychological lens that amplifies the translation of intention into sustained pro-environmental action, reinforcing long term sustainable consumption through repaired clothing practices. These argument lead to the assumption of following hypothesis:
H7. Behavioural intention to reuse has a positive effect on environmental sustainability.
H8. Self-identity has a positive effect on environmental sustainability.
H9. Self-identity moderates the relationship between behavioural intention to reuse and environmental sustainability.

3. Theoretical framework of the study
This study is grounded in consumption value theory, self-congruity theory, and sustainable consumption literature to explain consumer intention toward the use of repaired clothing and subsequent sustainable consumption behaviour. The theoretical framework (Figure 1) posits that tailoring skills and resources enhance consumer’s perceived behavioural control and repair self-efficacy, thereby facilitating repair oriented intentions (Ajzen, 1991). Replacement avoidance reflects anti-consumption and circular economy logics, where consumers intentionally resist disposal and new purchases to reduce environmental impact (Borusiak et al., 2020; Cooper, 2012). Hedonic motives capture the intrinsic pleasure, creativity, and enjoyment derived from repairing garments, which can positively shape pro-sustainable consumption choices (Hartmann & Apaolaza-Ibáñez, 2012). Nostalgic motives emphasize emotional connections to past experiences embedded in clothing, encouraging prolonged use and repair as a means of preserving personal memories (Holbrook & Schindler, 2003). Emotional attachment to clothing, often conceptualized as emotional durability, strengthens intentions to maintain and repair garments by linking apparel to identity, meaning, and psychological ownership (Chapman, 2009; Belk, 1988). Economic value further motivates repaired clothing use by highlighting cost savings and value maximization, particularly in resource conscious consumption contexts (Sheth et al., 1991). Collectively, these drivers influence consumer intention toward repaired clothing use, which in turn promotes sustainable consumption behaviour. Importantly, self-identity is theorized as a moderating variable, such that individuals with a strong sustainable or repair oriented self-identity are more likely to translate these drivers into repair intentions and sustainable behaviours, consistent with identity based motivation theory and self-congruity perspectives (Reed et al., 2012; Whitmarsh & O’Neill, 2010).

4. Research methodology
This study adopts a quantitative, cross-sectional research design to examine the determinants of behavioural intention to reuse clothing and the moderating role of self-identity among economically weaker sections (EWS) and small vendors residing in the Delhi NCR region. A structured, self-designed questionnaire was developed using validated scales adapted from prior sustainable consumption and reuse literature (Lang & Armstrong, 2018; Barbarossa & De Pelsmacker, 2016; Whitmarsh & O’Neill, 2010; Guiot & Roux, 2010), modified to suit the socioeconomic context of weaker sections and small vendors. The instrument consisted of three sections: demographic profile, determinants of reuse behaviour (measured using 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 = strongly disagree to 5 = strongly agree), and behavioural intention/self-identity measures. Self-Identity was operationalised as “green self-identity” or “sustainable consumer identity,” reflecting the degree to which individuals perceive themselves as environmentally responsible consumers (Whitmarsh & O’Neill, 2010). Data was collected through personal visits and face to face interactions to ensure clarity and inclusion of respondents with limited literacy levels. Descriptive statistics and questionnaire is presented in Appendix-A. A total of 600 questionnaires were distributed using purposive and convenience sampling techniques, out of which 531 valid responses were retained after screening for incomplete or inconsistent responses, yielding an effective response rate of 88.5%. Reliability of constructs was assessed using Cronbach’s alpha and Composite Reliability (CR), while convergent validity was established through Average Variance Extracted (AVE) and factor loadings (Hair et al., 2021). Discriminant validity was examined using the Fornell–Larcker criterion and HTMT ratio. Common Method Bias (CMB) was assessed through Harman’s single-factor test and full collinearity VIF values to ensure that no single factor explained the majority of variance (Podsakoff et al., 2003). Data analysis was conducted using SPSS (for descriptive statistics, exploratory factor analysis, and reliability testing) and SmartPLS 4 (for structural equation modeling, moderation analysis, and bootstrapping procedures). The structural model evaluated direct effects of reuse determinants on behavioural intention and the moderating effect of Self-Identity through interaction terms, with model fit assessed using R², f², Q², and SRMR values. The findings were interpreted to draw inferences regarding identity-driven sustainable consumption behaviour among economically constrained consumers and small vendors in urban informal sectors.

4.3 Structural model and hypotheses results
The structural model assessment demonstrates strong explanatory power, predictive relevance, and acceptable model fit. The R² value for BIR is 0.810 (adjusted R² = 0.808), indicating that 81% of the variance in behavioural intention is explained by its predictors, which exceeds the substantial threshold of 0.75 suggested by Hair et al. (2022) and reflects strong explanatory power. In contrast, ES shows an R² of 0.344 (adjusted R² = 0.340), representing moderate explanatory power, as values of 0.25, 0.50, and 0.75 are generally interpreted as weak, moderate, and substantial, respectively. The Q²predict values for BIR (0.805) and ES (0.402) are both greater than zero, confirming strong predictive relevance of the model according to the blindfolding criterion (Q² > 0). Additionally, the relatively low RMSE and MAE values indicate acceptable prediction errors. Regarding model fit, the SRMR values for both the saturated model (0.050) and the estimated model (0.059) fall below the recommended threshold of 0.08, indicating good model fit. The d_ULS (3.410) and d_G (2.278) values for the estimated model are close to those of the saturated model, further supporting model adequacy. Overall, the findings presented in Table 4 confirm that the structural model demonstrates substantial explanatory power, strong predictive relevance, and satisfactory global model fit.

4.4 Moderation analysis
The structural model results presented in Table 5 indicate that BIR has a strong and statistically significant positive effect on Environmental Sustainability (β = 0.539, STDEV = 0.044, t = 12.127, p = 0.000). The standardized path coefficient (β > 0.50) suggests a substantial positive influence, and the t-value far exceeds the recommended threshold of 1.96 for a 5% significance level (Hair et al., 2019), with p < 0.001 confirming high statistical significance. The effect size (f² = 0.354) indicates a large effect according to Cohen’s (1988) criteria (0.02 = small, 0.15 = medium, 0.35 = large). In contrast, the direct effect of Self-Identity on Environmental Sustainability is positive but statistically insignificant (β = 0.083, STDEV = 0.046, t = 1.813, p = 0.070), as the t-value falls below the 1.96 threshold and p > 0.05, suggesting that Self-Identity alone does not significantly predict Environmental Sustainability. However, the interaction term (Self-Identity × Behavioural Intention to Reuse) shows a statistically significant moderating effect (β = –0.093, STDEV = 0.045, t = 2.043, p = 0.041), with the t-value exceeding 1.96 and p < 0.05, thus supporting the moderation hypothesis. Although the interaction effect size is small (f² = 0.013, slightly below the 0.02 threshold for a small effect), the significant negative beta coefficient indicates that Self-Identity weakens the positive relationship between Behavioural Intention to Reuse and Environmental Sustainability. This suggests that the strength of the association between behavioural intention and sustainability outcomes varies depending on levels of self-identity, thereby confirming the presence of a statistically significant but modest moderating effect.

5. Discussions
The present study advances the growing discourse on sustainable consumption by demonstrating that clothing reuse behaviour is driven by a constellation of skill based, emotional, motivational, and value oriented factors, which collectively strengthen behavioural intention and, subsequently, environmental sustainability outcomes. Grounded in identity and consumption theory, the findings affirm that sustainable behaviour is not solely a rational economic choice but a multidimensional phenomenon shaped by personal capabilities, emotional bonds, and symbolic meanings attached to possessions (White et al., 2019; Belk, 2013).
First, tailoring skills and access to resources emerged as a dominant predictor of behavioural intention to reuse clothing. This finding aligns with competence based models of pro-environmental behaviour, which argue that individuals are more likely to engage in sustainable practices when they perceive themselves as capable and equipped (Bandura, 1997). Skill based empowerment enhances perceived behavioural control, thereby translating sustainable values into actionable behaviour. In the context of clothing reuse, practical knowledge such as repairing, altering, or creatively redesigning garments reduces dependency on new purchases and fosters a sense of autonomy and craftsmanship, reinforcing circular consumption patterns (Gwilt, 2020).
Replacement avoidance also significantly contributed to reuse intention, indicating that consumers who consciously resist frequent replacement cycles are more inclined toward sustainable alternatives. This supports literature on anti-consumption and sufficiency oriented lifestyles, which suggest that reducing material turnover is central to sustainability transitions (Jackson, 2021). Avoiding replacement reflects a shift from fast fashion norms toward durability oriented consumption, challenging dominant market logics of disposability (Niinimäki et al., 2020).
Hedonic and nostalgic motives further strengthened reuse intention, underscoring the importance of affective drivers in sustainable consumption. Contrary to the traditional assumption that sustainability is primarily morality driven, recent research emphasizes that pleasure, creativity, and enjoyment significantly enhance engagement in eco-friendly behaviours (Steg, 2023). Clothing reuse can provide intrinsic satisfaction through styling experimentation, personalization, and aesthetic reinvention. Similarly, nostalgia deepens attachment by linking garments to autobiographical memories, thereby extending product lifespan. Sentimental value transforms clothing from a functional commodity into a repository of identity and lived experience, reducing the likelihood of disposal.
Emotional attachment demonstrated a strong influence on reuse intention, reinforcing the theoretical premise that attachment prolongs product life cycles. Product attachment theory posits that emotionally bonded possessions are retained, repaired, and reused more frequently (Mugge, 2017). Emotional durability has thus been recognized as a strategic lever for sustainable fashion, as consumers are less likely to discard items that hold personal significance (Chapman, 2015). The present findings corroborate this perspective, highlighting emotional bonds as a powerful mechanism in strengthening reuse behaviour.
Economic value, while positively associated with reuse intention, exerted comparatively less influence than emotional and skill based factors. This suggests that although cost saving considerations remains relevant, reuse behaviour is not exclusively financially motivated. Recent sustainability research indicates that pro-environmental consumption increasingly reflects identity expression and moral alignment rather than mere economic rationality (Hampton & Whitmarsh, 2023; Trudel, 2019). Thus, clothing reuse appears to be embedded in broader symbolic and experiential dimensions beyond cost efficiency.
Importantly, behavioural intention to reuse demonstrated a strong contribution to environmental sustainability outcomes, reinforcing the central premise of the Theory of Planned Behaviour that intention is a proximal determinant of actual behaviour (Ajzen, 2020). When consumers exhibit strong intention to extend garment lifespan, cumulative environmental benefits such as reduced textile waste, lower carbon emissions, and decreased resource extraction become more attainable (Dragomir & Dumitru, 2022). This underscores the policy and managerial implication that strengthening reuse intention is critical for advancing circular fashion systems.
The findings reveal a complex and dual role of self-identity in sustainable repaired clothing consumption. Although self-identity did not independently predict environmental sustainability outcomes in all models, it demonstrated both a strong direct association with sustainability orientation and a significant negative moderating effect on the relationship between reuse intention and sustainable behaviour. Consistent with identity based consumption and self-congruity theories, individuals who perceive sustainability as central to their self-concept are more likely to internalize pro-environmental values, support circular fashion principles, and endorse garment longevity (Hampton & Whitmarsh, 2023; Barbarossa & De Pelsmacker, 2016; van der Werff et al., 2014). In the context of repaired clothing, sustainability oriented self-identity encourages symbolic alignment with ethical consumption, emotional attachment to garments, and moral satisfaction derived from extending product lifecycles (Niinimäki et al., 2020; Armstrong et al., 2015). These findings confirm that self-identity operates as a foundational psychological driver of sustainability consciousness.
However, the negative moderating effect of self-identity on the intention–behaviour relationship suggests an intention–behaviour decoupling among individuals with highly salient sustainability identities. One plausible explanation lies in moral licensing and identity saturation mechanisms, whereby strongly identified “sustainable consumers” may experience moral self-completion; merely endorsing sustainable intentions symbolically affirms their identity, thereby reducing the urgency to translate intention into consistent behavioural action (Truelove et al., 2014; Merritt et al., 2010; Mazar & Zhong, 2010). Additionally, identity salience may heighten sensitivity to symbolic and aesthetic standards. Although repaired clothing is cognitively valued, it may conflict with aspirations related to professionalism, modernity, or social status, creating identity dissonance in socially visible contexts (Wiederhold & Martinez, 2018; Bly et al., 2015; Joy et al., 2012). Persistent stigma surrounding visibly mended garments may further inhibit enactment among identity-driven consumers (Han et al., 2017; McNeill & Moore, 2015). From a self-regulation perspective, strong sustainability identities may also foster overconfidence in moral consistency, weakening effortful behavioural follow-through (Lacasse, 2019; Thøgersen & Crompton, 2009). Thus, self-identity functions simultaneously as a motivational driver and a psychological shortcut, potentially substituting symbolic affirmation for consistent sustainable practice (White et al., 2019; Carrington et al., 2014).
Thus, the findings partially support identity based theories of sustainable consumption but also caution against assuming that identity uniformly amplifies environmental outcomes. The modest magnitude of the moderating effect indicates that self-identity shapes the strength of intention–behaviour linkages but does not override the influence of practical skills, emotional attachment, and motivational drivers. This aligns with contemporary perspectives suggesting that identity must be supported by enabling structures, competencies, and emotional engagement to produce sustained behavioural change (Steg, 2023). Overall, the study contributes to sustainable consumption literature by demonstrating that clothing reuse behaviour is primarily reinforced by competence, emotional durability, nostalgic meaning, and intrinsic enjoyment, while economic incentives play a secondary role. Although self-identity remains an important contextual factor, its influence appears complex and conditional. These insights underscore the need for integrative interventions that combine skill development, emotional design strategies, and identity based communication to foster durable and scalable sustainable consumption practices.

6. Study Implications
6.1 Theoretical contribution
The present study makes a significant theoretical contribution to sustainable consumption scholarship by integrating identity based theory with competence, emotional durability, and sufficiency-oriented perspectives to explain clothing reuse behaviour as a multidimensional and identity embedded process. Drawing on identity and consumption theory (Hampton & Whitmarsh, 2023; Belk, 2013), the findings extend the TPB (Ajzen, 2020) by demonstrating that reuse intention is not merely shaped by attitudinal evaluation but is strengthened through skill based empowerment consistent with Social Cognitive Theory (Bandura, 1997) and perceived behavioural control mechanisms. By highlighting tailoring skills and access to resources as dominant predictors, the study advances competence-based models of pro-environmental action and aligns with circular fashion scholarship emphasizing consumer craftsmanship and autonomy (Gwilt, 2020). Furthermore, the significant roles of hedonic enjoyment, nostalgia, and emotional attachment expand product attachment theory and emotional durability frameworks (Mugge, 2017; Chapman, 2015) by positioning affective bonds and autobiographical meaning as central drivers of extended product lifecycles. The study also contributes to anti-consumption and sufficiency literature (Jackson, 2021; Niinimäki et al., 2020) by empirically confirming replacement avoidance as a behavioural mechanism challenging fast fashion disposability norms. Importantly, it refines identity based theories of sustainable consumption (Barbarossa & De Pelsmacker, 2016; Van der Werff et al., 2014) by uncovering the dual and conditional role of sustainability self-identity. While identity strengthens sustainability orientation and symbolic alignment, its negative moderation of the intention–behaviour relationship reveals potential moral licensing and identity saturation effects (Truelove et al., 2014; Merritt et al., 2010), thereby contributing to the intention–behaviour gap literature (White et al., 2019; Carrington et al., 2014). By demonstrating that identity alone does not guarantee behavioural enactment without supportive competencies and emotional engagement (Steg, 2023), the study advances a more integrative theoretical model in which competence, affect, symbolic meaning, and identity dynamically interact to produce durable sustainable consumption outcomes, thus enriching contemporary debates on circular economy transitions and identity-driven pro-environmental behaviour.

6.2 Managerial implications
The findings provide actionable insights for fashion brands, policymakers, sustainability advocates, and circular economy practitioners seeking to promote clothing reuse behaviour. First, organizations should prioritize skill based empowerment initiatives, such as repair workshops, DIY alteration tutorial, accessible tailoring services, and community mending events as competence significantly enhances reuse intention. Facilitating consumer capability reduces perceived barriers and translates sustainability values into practical action. Second, brands should design products that foster emotional durability by encouraging personalization, storytelling, and aesthetic reinvention, thereby strengthening emotional attachment and nostalgic value. Marketing strategies should emphasize creativity, enjoyment, and self expression rather than solely moral appeals, as hedonic and sentimental motives were stronger predictors than economic incentives. Third, communication campaigns should carefully frame sustainability identity to avoid moral licensing effects; instead of relying solely on symbolic identity affirmation (e.g., labelling consumers as “eco-conscious”), interventions should emphasize consistent behavioural follow-through and normalize repaired clothing in socially visible contexts to reduce stigma. Retailers can also elevate the aesthetic appeal of repaired or upcycled garments through design innovation, influencer endorsement, and premium positioning to mitigate identity dissonance. Finally, policymakers should support circular infrastructure by subsidizing repair services, integrating repair education into curricula, and incentivizing durability focused production systems. Collectively, these strategies suggest that sustainable fashion transitions require a blend of skill development, emotional engagement, identity sensitive communication, and structural support mechanisms.

7. Conclusions, limitations and future research directions of the study
In conclusion, this study demonstrates that identity-driven sustainable consumption in the context of clothing reuse is fundamentally shaped by interplay of competence, emotional attachment, nostalgic meaning, intrinsic enjoyment, and sufficiency oriented motivations. While economic value contributes modestly, sustainable reuse behaviour is predominantly anchored in experiential and symbolic dimensions rather than purely financial considerations. Behavioural intention remains a strong predictor of environmental sustainability outcomes, reaffirming the centrality of intention in pro-environmental behaviour models. However, the dual role of self-identity reveals a more complex dynamic: although sustainability oriented identity strengthens environmental orientation, it may weaken the translation of intention into action under certain psychological conditions, reflecting moral licensing and identity based self-regulation processes. These findings highlight that sustainable behaviour cannot be assumed to follow automatically from identity endorsement; instead, identity must be supported by competencies, emotional engagement, and enabling contexts. By offering an integrative understanding of clothing reuse behaviour, the study contributes to advancing circular fashion systems and broadens theoretical discourse on sustainable consumption.
Despite its contributions, the study has several limitations. First, the cross-sectional research design restricts causal inference and limits the ability to capture dynamic changes in identity salience and reuse behaviour over time. Longitudinal designs would provide stronger evidence regarding the persistence of intention–behaviour relationships. Second, reliance on self reported measures may introduce social desirability bias, particularly in sustainability research where moral norms are salient. Third, the study’s contextual focus may limit generalizability across different cultural, socioeconomic, and fashion market environments, as clothing symbolism and repair norms vary substantially across societies. Fourth, while the moderating effect of self-identity was statistically significant, its modest magnitude suggests the presence of additional unexamined contextual or psychological variables, such as social norms, perceived stigma, aesthetic concerns, or habit strength. Finally, actual behavioural outcomes were inferred primarily through intention measures, and future research should incorporate objective or behavioural tracking indicators to validate real world reuse practices.
Future research should adopt longitudinal and experimental designs to better understand the temporal dynamics of sustainability identity and potential moral licensing effects in clothing reuse behaviour. Investigating mediating mechanisms such as perceived stigma, aesthetic evaluation, social visibility, and moral self regulation could clarify why strong sustainability identities sometimes weaken behavioural enactment. Cross-cultural comparative studies would also be valuable in examining how identity salience interacts with cultural norms, fashion systems, and economic contexts. Additionally, future studies may integrate social influence variables, such as peer norms and digital community engagement, to explore collective identity formation in circular fashion movements. Employing mixed method approaches, including qualitative interviews and behavioural experiments, could deepen insights into symbolic meanings and identity negotiations surrounding repaired clothing. Finally, expanding the framework to other circular consumption domains, such as resale, sharing platforms and upcycling would enhance the generalizability of the proposed identity-driven sustainable consumption model and contribute to broader sustainability transition research.
