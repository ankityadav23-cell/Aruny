Examining adoption behaviour towards AI-enabled educational services: Extending the theory of trying with technological readiness as a moderating boundary condition

Abstract
The rapid integration of artificial intelligence into higher education has generated considerable optimism, yet learner frequently fails to convert favourable dispositions toward AI-enabled educational services into sustained engagement. The study aims to examine the transition of learners’ intention to try into adoption behaviour of AI-enabled educational services, applying Theory of Trying. Drawing on a cross-sectional survey of 741 students in higher education institutes of Delhi-NCR, study employed partial least squares structural equation modelling (PLS-SEM). The study finds that attitude toward failure exerts the strongest effect on intention to try, consistent with loss-aversion mechanisms theorised in the ToT and prospect theory. Attitude toward success, attitude toward process, social norms, and perceived behavioural control all exert significant positive effects on intention to try. Intention to try further strongly predicts the adoption behaviour. Technological readiness exerts a modest negative direct effect on adoption behaviour, while its positive moderating effect on the intention to try and adoption behaviour relationship confirms that technological readiness amplifies the translation of motivated striving into adoption behaviour. The study findings validate the ToT as a theoretically precise lens for AI adoption in education, extend it by integrating technological readiness as a moderator, and yield actionable guidance for educational administrators, EdTech developers, and policymakers seeking to bridge the intention-adoption gap in AI-augmented learning contexts.
Keywords: Attitude, AI, EdTech, Learners, Technology

1. Introduction
Artificial intelligence (AI) has transitioned from a marginal technological curiosity to a structural force reshaping the architecture of higher education system and resulting into development of many learning platforms (Temimi et al., 2025). Learning platforms calibrate content difficulty to learners’ trajectory in real time. These includes, intelligent tutoring systems providing personalised pedagogical scaffolding, AI-driven assessment engines to evaluate complex outputs, and conversational pedagogical agents for sustained engagement and interaction (Gligorea et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2021). Considering such AI interventions, global investment in AI infrastructure is projected to reach USD 285.2 billion by 2027. This signifies the strategic consensus about the centrality of AI to the future of global education (Sethi et al., 2023). Sheeran and Webb (2016) and Nurtanto et al. (2025) are of the view that a substantial proportion of learners express intention to use AI-enabled educational services, but fail to convert such intentions into adoption. The reason for this is complex and uncertain behavioural characteristics of AI tool adoption (Sheeran and Webb, 2016; Gidaković et al., 2024). It is also evident from the extant literature that behavioural intention explains around fifty percent of variance in adoption behaviour. Thus, the gap between intention and adoption has remained unresolved to date.  
The technology acceptance model (TAM; Davis, 1989) and the unified theory of acceptance and use of technology (UTAUT; Venkatesh et al., 2003), the dominant frameworks to explain technology adoption behaviour, are structurally inadequate for explaining the gap between intention and adoption. Both frameworks act as an intention-driven process where positive attitude formation precedes and causes behavioural enactment, treating intention-to-behaviour transition as automatic rather than a volitional challenge (Bagozzi, 2007). Critically speaking, TAM and UTAUT both focus mainly on the positive factors for technology adoption process and ignore the negative ones. Moreover, these models assume that barriers to adoption are simply the absence of positive attitudes, rather than recognizing that negative expectations can independently discourage individuals from adopting a technology. Consequently, these models haven’t been found sufficient in explaining the adequate reason for the intention-behaviour gap amongst the users.
Adoption of AI in education sector is not a single decision, it’s an iterative process of trial, error, failure, and persistence (Ali et al., 2024). No framework has been effectively able to capture the experiential dynamics of this iterative process (Dwivedi et al., 2021; Gidaković et al., 2024). The theory of trying (ToT; Bagozzi and Warshaw, 1990) was developed precisely to address the intention–behaviour gap in contexts where goal pursuit is full of efforts, uncertain, and susceptible to failure. By reconceptualising adoption as a striving process, this theory disaggregates the unidimensional attitude towards behaviour construct into three functionally distinct attitudinal dimensions (Bagozzi and Warshaw, 1990). These three dimensions are: attitude towards the process of trying (ATP), attitude toward the outcomes of successful attempts (ATS), and attitude toward the outcomes of unsuccessful attempts (ATF). This tripartite architecture makes failure attitudes theoretically operative and positions the theory of trying as superior in explaining adoption in domains defined by complexity, uncertainty, and iterative experimentation. Despite this advantage, the theory of trying has received limited empirical application in technology adoption contexts. This study directly focuses on the application of this theory to AI-enabled educational services and fills the long awaiting gaps persisting in the published literature. To achieve the aim, author(s) have developed an integrated conceptual framework.  
The proposed conceptual framework is enriched by integrating two complementary theories: the theory of planned behaviour (TPB) and the theory of trying (ToT). Social norms (SNO) and perceived behavioural control (PBC) have been taken from the TPB (Ajzen, 1991) as operationalisation of the normative and capability dimensions of trial intentions that the ToT references, but do not theorise fully. Technological readiness (TRE) has been taken from the technology readiness theory (TRT; Parasuraman, 2000; Parasuraman and Colby, 2015) that contributes as a dispositional enabling variable. Its moderating effect in the conversion of trial intentions into adoption behaviour has been proposed. This specifies the ability-based boundary condition under which motivational commitment reliably produces sustained use.
The study has been contextualised in India as the higher education system of India is the world's second largest, enrolling over 43 million students (Sharma et al., 2024). The Delhi-NCR region of India combines rapid deployment of AI tools across institutions with significant socio-economic heterogeneity of learners’ background. India's collectivist cultural orientation amplifies normative adoption mechanisms, providing optimal conditions for testing the social influence pathways (Shneor et al., 2021).
Majorly, the study identifies four specific research gaps to investigate. First, existing frameworks systematically exclude failure attitude as independent motivational construct, producing models that can predict adoption from positive dispositions and from failures arising from active inhibitory forces. Second, the intention-behaviour link for complex, uncertain and iterative adoption processes requires mechanism-specific solutions, not automatic assumptions. Third, the theory of trying empirically excludes AI-enabled educational services despite satisfying all its boundary conditions. Lastly, the moderating role of technological readiness in the intention-adoption conversion has been theoretically proposed but never tested empirically within the framework of the theory of trying.
Based on the gaps, the study tries to guide its enquiry through following research questions:
RQ1: How tripartite attitudinal constructs of the theory of trying together with social norms and perceived behavioural control collectively determine intention to try AI-enabled educational services?
RQ2: To what extent intentions to try translate into adoption behaviour?
RQ3: How technological readinesses shape the intention-adoption conversion?
Based on the empirical findings, the study makes the following novel contributions to the existing literature. First, it provides inaugural empirical validation of the theory of trying in AI-enabled educational services. Second, it empirically demonstrates the loss-aversion asymmetry through which ATF exerts disproportionate inhibitory effects, a finding that TAM and UTAUT structurally can’t replicate. Third, it establishes TRT as a theoretically coherent boundary condition within the ToT framework. Fourth, it documents the sophistication effect that high TRE suppresses direct adoption while amplifies motivationally-driven adoption. Lastly, it demonstrates that tri-theoretic integration (ToT+TPB+TRT) produces superior explanatory specification compared to a single-framework alternative.
The whole paper has been organised in eight sections. Establishing the context of the study in the first section, theoretical architecture has been developed in the succeeding section. Section 3 includes intensive review of the related literature which helped us in developing the hypotheses using four-level funnel. Section 4 of the study describes the research methodology followed by analysis and interpretation of results in the next section. A detailed discussion has been presented in section 6 followed by major contributions of the study in the second last section. The last section gives details about the limitations and future directions which emerged from the study.

2. Theoretical framework
2.1 Competing frameworks and elimination
TAM (Davis, 1989) operationalises adoption through perceived usefulness and ease of use. Cognitive evaluations of task-fit and operational efficiency are appropriate for routine IS (Information System) where adoption is a utility judgment, not a volitional striving process. For AI-enabled educational services, where adoption involves iterative experimentation with uncertain outcomes and genuine failure risk, TAM's two-construct cognitive evaluations provide necessary but structurally insufficient explanatory coverage. TAM cannot account for why learners, who evaluate an AI tool as both useful and easy, fail to adopt it. Precisely, this study strives to investigate this phenomenon. UTAUT (Venkatesh et al., 2003) is an extension of TAM with social influence and facilitating conditions. But this model retains the smooth intention-behaviour assumption and excludes failure-anticipatory affect as an independent motivational variable. The Heuristic-Systematic Model (Chaiken, 1980) addresses dual-route processing but do not operationalise goal-directed striving. Another model i.e. Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers, 2003) operates at an aggregate population level and provides no individual motivational mechanism. The systematic elimination of failure in all these models establishes the theory of trying (ToT) as the theoretically mandated primary framework for the adoption challenge under investigation.

2.2 Theory of trying as primary explanatory architecture
Bagozzi and Warshaw developed the theory of trying (ToT) in 1990 to address the motivational misspecification of the theory of reasoned action. The single attitude-toward-behaviour construct collapsed the experiential complexity of effortful, uncertain, and failure-susceptible goal pursuit into a single evaluative dimension. The causal mechanism of ToT operates through three functionally distinct attitudinal forces. First, attitude toward process (ATP) captures the intrinsic motivational valence of trying experience, operationalising whether act of engaging with a novel technology is inherently worthwhile and independent of outcomes. Its causal role is approach-motivational. Positive ATP lowers the activation threshold for initiating trying. Second, attitude toward success (ATS) captures positive affective appraisal of anticipated successful outcomes by providing anticipated rewards that sustains effortful engagement through difficulty. Third, attitude toward failure (ATF) captures negative affective evaluation of unsuccessful outcome anticipation by activating an avoidance-motivation pathway that competes directly with the approach motivations of ATP and ATS. The critical advancement of the ToT is that it specifies ATF as an independent motivational force with its psychological mechanism and intervention requirements. The boundary conditions of the theory of trying are theoretically precise: (a) uncertain behavioural outcomes, (b) effortful trying preceding outcome achievement, (c) failure as a psychologically significant possibility, and (d) iterative trial-and-evaluation rather than single-shot execution. AI-educational adoption satisfies all four conditions with theoretical precision and making the ToT not merely relevant but theoretically mandated for this context (Bagozzi, 2007; Dwivedi et al., 2021).

2.3 Theory of planned behaviour as normative and capability supplement
The TPB (Ajzen, 1991) contributes SNO and PBC as construct-level supplements for the normative and capability dimensions of trial intentions that the theory of trying references, but do not operationalise fully. SNO captures both injunctive norms (perceived social pressure) and descriptive norms (perceptions of referent behaviour) and provides dual-mechanism normative coverage. PBC captures internal control beliefs (self-efficacy) and external facilitating conditions (resource availability) and provides theoretically complete capability specification. For the above discussed reasons, the TPB is chosen over UTAUT as it provides direct theoretical antecedent of the ToT (Bagozzi and Warshaw, 1990). This makes the integration theoretically coherent rather than eclectic.

2.4 Technology readiness theory as dispositional moderating boundary condition
Technology readiness theory (Parasuraman, 2000; Parasuraman and Colby, 2015) conceptualises technology readiness (TRE) as a stable dispositional propensity towards new technologies operationalised through optimism and innovativeness as facilitating dimensions. TRE is theoretically distinct from situational perceptions of TAM and attitudinal constructs of ToT. It’s a cross-situational enabling characteristic and not a technology-specific evaluation. The moderating mechanism derives from Bandura's (1986) social cognitive interaction reflects that behavioural outcomes reflect the interaction between motivational states and enabling personal characteristics. TRE amplifies the ITT-to-adoption conversion by providing the dispositional capability and optimism needed to navigate adoption barriers that interrupt motivational conversion for low-TRE learners.
The sophistication effect considering the predicted negative direct TRE effect on adoption when ITT is controlled theoretically grounded in two complementary mechanisms. Technically capable learners apply more stringent pedagogical effectiveness and algorithmic reliability criteria before committing to sustained use (Dwivedi et al., 2021). Within the ToT, this corresponds to technically ready learners attempting AI tools, evaluating them against high standards, finding threshold performance unmet, and withholding sustained use, precisely what the ToT predicts for trying that produces outcome evaluation rather than automatic adoption. The moderating interaction resolves this when high TRE co-occurs with strong ITT, the sophistication-driven evaluation threshold is met, and motivational conversion is amplified rather than suppressed.

2.5 Integrated theoretical logic
The three frameworks answer sequentially distinct explanatory questions within a coherent integrated architecture. The theory of trying specifies motivational structure of trial intention formation where five forces (ATP, ATS, ATF, SNO, PBC) determine strength of intention to try (ITT) through distinct causal pathways. ITT is the proximal motivational antecedent of adoption behaviour, capturing volitional commitment to the striving process. TRE moderates the ITT-to-adoption conversion efficiency and constitutes dispositional enabling condition for motivational enactment. Together, they generate a theoretically complete causal chain from motivational inputs to dispositional conditioned adoption behaviour. No single prior framework provides this complete specification and even no previous studies have tested the integration of theory of trying and technology readiness theory (ToT+TRT) in the AI-educational context.

3. Literature review
3.1 AI integration and the adoption challenge in higher education
The integration of AI into higher education represents a structural transformation with both immense pedagogical promise and persistent adoption resistance (Qian et al., 2025). Globally, AI tools offer documented benefits including personalised learning trajectories, real-time feedback, and adaptive assessment calibration (Gligorea et al., 2023). Despite such benefits, learners frequently fail to convert positive AI evaluations into sustained use (Sheeran and Webb, 2016; Nurtanto et al., 2025). Review of digital media effects by Boulianne (2020), meta-analysis of intention–behaviour gaps by Sheeran and Webb (2016), and study of sustainable consumption intentions by Gidaković et al. (2024) collectively establish that this gap is not random noise but systematic. 
Indian context amplifies these challenges. While the National Education Policy 2020 mandates AI integration, the country's significant heterogeneity in digital literacy, socioeconomic backgrounds, and institutional AI readiness makes India a natural laboratory for testing the capability and readiness of the proposed framework (Sharma et al., 2024; Kampa, 2023). The collectivist educational culture of India amplifies normative adoption pathways (Shneor et al., 2021), while the wider distributional spread of technological sophistication amongst students maximises the detectability of the moderation effect of technological readiness (TRE).

3.2 Mechanism-level adoption dynamics
Mechanism-level examination of prior technology adoption studies reveals the structural inadequacy of positive-valence frameworks for uncertain adoption contexts (Erdmann & Toro-Dupouy, 2025). It has been duly documented by Jeyaraj et al. (2023) that conversion rates of intention-to-behaviour in IS adoption context falls consistently below the predictions of TAM and UTAUT. This shortfall has been reported highest in complex and voluntary adoptions. Sheeran and Webb (2016) have identified the failure risk and outcome uncertainty as the primary gap-amplifying conditions that characterise AI-educational adoption. This analysis has been extended by Gidaković et al. (2024) by demonstrating that failure-anticipatory attitudes are independent and substantial predictors of sustainable consumption intentions. Further they concluded that such intentions are directly parallel to AI adoption context in establishing cross-domain generalisability of loss-aversion mechanism.
The AI adoption domain specifically exhibits conditions under which positive-valence frameworks severely underperform. Dwivedi et al. (2021) and Holmes (2020) in their studies observed that algorithmic outputs are uncertain and context-dependent, effective usage requires skill development, failure consequences are educationally meaningful, and adoption emerges through iterative evaluation rather than single decision execution. These four characteristics collectively constitute the mandated application boundary conditions of the theory of trying. This not only establishes the relevance of the theory of trying, but its theoretical necessity for this domain.
Figure 1 presents the integrated conceptual framework. Five antecedents of the integrated ToT-TPB framework: three from the theory of trying (ATP, ATS, ATF) and two from the theory of planned behaviour (SNO and PBC) collectively determine intention to try (ITT) through theoretically specified causal pathways. ITT is the proximal predictor of Adoption Behaviour (ABE) representing the striving-process of ToT for conversion from committed motivation to enacted adoption. TRE constitutes dispositional enabling boundary condition, enters the model both as a direct predictor of ABE and as a moderator of the ITT-ABE conversion.

3.3 Hypothesis development
3.3.1 Attitude toward process and intention to try
ATP captures learners' intrinsic evaluative orientation toward the act of experimenting with AI-enabled educational services. Such orientation is independent of anticipated outcomes whether navigating AI interfaces, interpreting algorithmic recommendations, and customising learning pathways are found inherently engaging or personally enriching (Bagozzi and Warshaw, 1990). ATP finds theoretical grounding in Self-Determination Theory (SDT; Deci and Ryan, 2012), which establishes that behaviour experienced as intrinsically satisfying and autonomy-supporting are more reliably initiated and sustained than those motivated purely by extrinsic consequences. When the trying process is valued, learners develop a motivational buffer against early difficulties and they persist through initial complexity because the engagement is rewarding. Chen et al. (2025) observed that process-related positive affect significantly predicts trial intentions, especially in educational technology contexts, whereas Daly et al. (2025) find that it buffers the inhibitory effects of outcome uncertainty on intention formation. Glassberg et al. (2025) in their study document that intrinsic engagement orientation predicts persistence through initial difficulties of AI tools. Based on literature support, the following hypothesis has been formulated.
H1: Attitude toward process positively influences learners' intention to try AI-enabled educational services.

3.3.2 Attitude toward success and intention to try
ATS denotes learners' positive affective appraisal of outcomes, anticipated out of successful engagements, in the form of improvement in academic performance, enhanced learning efficiency, personalised skill development, and self-confidence (Liang and Reiss, 2025). Unlike perceived usefulness of TAM, ATS captures the emotional significance and personal value attached to learners’ prospective success, not merely cognitive outcome probability estimation (Gidaković et al., 2024). Expectancy-value theory (Wigfield and Eccles, 2000) posits that motivation enhances the probability of subjective success when combined with subjective outcome value. In AI-educational contexts, it has been significantly established that prospective gains from successful use (personalised feedback, adaptive difficulty, and real-time learning analytics) are cognitively salient for academically motivated learners (Feng et al., 2025; Cheng et al., 2021). Bandura's (2014) social cognitive theory also predicts that positive outcome expectations foster self-regulatory persistence and reinforces motivational contribution through resilience amplification during the process of trying. These arguments lead to development of the second hypothesis.
H2: Attitude toward success positively influences learners' intention to try AI-enabled educational services.

3.3.3 Attitude toward failure and intention to try
ATF represents learners' negative affective evaluation of outcomes anticipated from unsuccessful attempts. Such evaluations may be in the form of algorithmic error, data privacy violations, reduced human interaction, and academic consequences of dependence on AI tools (Holmes, 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). ATF operates through an avoidance-motivation pathway that is independent of and not reducible to the absence of ATS (Bagozzi, 1992). This independence is the most theoretically distinctive contribution of the theory of trying. Prospect theory (Kahneman and Tversky, 2013) provides the psychological mechanism. It propounds that loss aversion predicts that psychological cost of anticipated failure systematically exceeds the equivalent psychological benefit of anticipated success. Further, it produces asymmetric attitudinal influence that TAM and UTAUT are structurally unable to detect. Research on educational psychology confirms that fear of failure (as a discrete motivational construct) suppresses exploratory learning behaviour, especially in context of AI adoption (Conroy, 2004). In educational contexts, the opacity of AI decision-making worsens the attitude towards failure when learners are unable to understand how an algorithm assigned leads to a particular performance level.  Such perceived inability to understand and recover from negative outcomes amplifies failure-related anxiety of the learners (Gidaković et al., 2024). This asymmetry (ATF > ATS) has been reported in financial technology adoption which is a structurally analogous complex technological context. These arguments lead to the development of third hypothesis.  
H3: Attitude toward failure negatively influences learners' intention to try AI-enabled educational services.

3.3.4 Social norms and intention to try
SNO captures perceived social pressure from others to perform a particular behaviour (Ajzen, 1991). In AI-educational context, three normative mechanisms operate. First, injunctive norms (explicit social approval from institutions) confer legitimacy on the AI experimentation through the desire of social acceptance (Cialdini and Goldstein, 2004). Second, descriptive norms (perceptions of peer for AI adoption) reduce perceived trial risk through social proof (Bandura, 2001). Third, image effects (the perceived impact for being a technically capable student) amplify normative pressure in competitive academic environments. In India's collectivist educational culture, these mechanisms carry heightened motivational salience. It has been established that cross-cultural research consistently documents stronger normative influence in collectivist than individualist societies (Shneor et al., 2021). In Asian higher education contexts, the significance of social and institutional norms for educational technology adoption intentions has been significantly established by Feng et al. (2025). Therefore, the fourth hypothesis can be developed. 
H4: Social norms positively influence learners' intention to try AI-enabled educational services.

3.3.5 Perceived behavioural control and intention to try
PBC captures both internal control beliefs reflecting self-efficacy (Bandura, 1978) and external facilitating conditions encompassing resource access (Ajzen, 1991; Compeau and Higgins, 1995). PBC is theoretically critical in AI-educational adoption for three reasons. First, technical complexity of AI systems creates genuine capability uncertainty and suppresses trial intentions for learners who lack prior experiences with algorithmic personalisation (Nurtanto et al., 2025). Second, access to enabling infrastructure constitutes an external dimension of PBC, substantially varying across socioeconomic educational landscape of India (Rana et al., 2025). Third, social cognitive theory (Bandura, 2014) predicts that high self-efficacy fosters exploratory behaviour persistence through difficulty and resilience. Such characteristics are directly theorised in the theory of trying as components of effective striving. Meta-analysis by Sharma et al. (2025) and Jiao and Cao (2024) confirm that PBC and self-efficacy are consistently large-magnitude predictors of technology adoption intentions in education. This leads to establishment of fifth hypothesis.
H5: Perceived behavioural control positively influences learners' intention to try AI-enabled educational services.

3.3.6 Intention to try and adoption behaviour
ITT occupies the pivotal position in the theory of trying which distinguishes it from general behavioural intention constructs of TAM and UTAUT. Conventional intention constructs capture the cognitive decision to adopt, whereas the ITT captures the volitional commitment to engage in effortful, iterative, and potentially unsuccessful process of trying. This distinction is theoretically crucial and acknowledges that the pathway from motivation to adoption involves genuine striving and the quality of motivational commitment determines that the behaviour is enacted (Bagozzi and Warshaw, 1990). Trialability construct by Rogers (2003) converges on this insight that innovations that learners can experiment diffuse more rapidly. This happens because the trial process reduces uncertainty, builds competence and escalates commitment. Jeyaraj et al. (2023) confirm that intention remains the most robust proximal predictor of adoption behaviour across information science contexts. Chaouali et al. (2017) in their study specifically demonstrate that trial-based ITT of ToT predicts adoption with elevated precision in complex technological contexts. This helps in reaching to the sixth hypothesis.
H6: Intention to try AI-enabled educational services positively influences adoption behaviour.

3.3.7 Technological readiness: Direct and moderating effects
Technology readiness (TRE) is a stable and having cross-situational dispositional propensity toward new technologies (Parasuraman, 2000; Parasuraman and Colby, 2015). This is distinct from situational perceptions of TAM and technology-specific attitudinal evaluations of ToT. Its multidimensional architecture captures dispositional openness, enthusiasm, and pioneering tendency toward technology adoption. The moderating effect of TRE on ITT-to-adoption conversion derives from Bandura's (1986) social cognitive interaction which outlines that behavioural outcomes reflect the interaction of motivational states with enabling personal characteristics. TRE amplifies conversion by providing dispositional capability, optimism, and digital environment competence needed to navigate the experiential barriers interrupting motivated adoption for low-TRE learners (Ayanwale and Ndlovu, 2024; Han et al., 2024). UTAUT2 propounded by Venkatesh et al. (2012) also converges on this prediction through facilitating conditions of moderation. Simultaneous negative direct TRE effect is predicted on a priori by the sophistication mechanism. Dwivedi et al. (2021) are of the view that technically ready learners, who lack strong trial motivation, apply stringent evaluative criteria before committing to sustained use. Such learners withhold adoption for satisfactory pedagogical performance evidences. When high TRE and high ITT occur simultaneously, the sophistication threshold is met and conversion is amplified rather than suppressed. This argument leads to development of seventh hypothesis of the study.
H7: Technological readiness positively moderates the relationship between intention to try and adoption behaviour.

4. Research methodology
4.1 Research Design
This study adopts a post-positivist ontological position and a hypothetico-deductive epistemological approach (Creswell and Creswell, 2017). A post-positivist ontological position defines that objective reality exists, but not understood perfectly due to human limitations and a hypothetico-deductive epistemological approach helps in understanding such reasons through empirical observation and revising theories. A quantitative cross-sectional survey was employed which is consistent with other intention-adoption researches done by Chaouali et al. (2017) and Issock et al. (2021) related to theory of trying, Teo (2011) related to theory of planned behaviour, and Parasuraman and Colby (2015) related to technology readiness theory. This enables the study at par with other methodological comparisons across theoretical traditions related to technology adoption. Theoretically derived hypotheses are tested against the data collected, analysed, and interpreted as probabilistic evidence for or against theoretical propositions.
 
4.2 Measurement instrument
A structured questionnaire with five-point Likert scales (1=Strongly Disagree to 5=Strongly Agree) has been used for all items. Three measurement constructs: ATP, ATS, and ATF were adapted from Mazı and Mazı (2025), Suh and Ahn (2022), and Wang and Wang (2022). This corroborates with the measurement philosophy of Bagozzi and Warshaw's (1990). Next two constructs: SNO and PBC were adapted from Jiao and Cao (2024), Wang et al. (2025), and Ajzen (1991). The construct i.e. ITT was adapted from Venkatesh et al. (2003) and Davis (1989) and modified to foreground trial commitment rather than general usage intention. TRE has been used as the optimism and innovativeness sub-scales of TRI 2.0 (Parasuraman and Colby, 2015). ABE was operationalised from Alotaibi (2026) for capturing frequency, continuity, and depth of engagement. Content validity of the instrument was confirmed through expert reviews from academic experts in educational technology and behavioural research. A pilot study with 60 students yielded all significant results with Cronbach's alpha values exceeding 0.70 (Nunnally and Bernstein, 1994).

4.3 Research sample
The target population comprised of students enrolled in higher education institutions across Delhi-NCR, India. The eligibility criterion is that respondents should have at least some prior exposure to or awareness of AI-enabled educational services. This is needed as a prerequisite for meaningful evaluation of attitudinal construct of the theory of trying. Delhi-NCR was selected for three theoretical reasons. First, its socioeconomic diversity maximises variance on PBC and TRE which is crucial for detecting construct-level variance independently. Second, deployment of AI tools across heterogeneous institutions which create genuine variation in normative environments and prior exposure to AI. Lastly, India's collectivist cultural orientation amplifies effects of subjective norms and provides optimal conditions for detecting normative adoption pathways (Shneor et al., 2021). A purposive non-probability sampling strategy ensured eligible respondents. Data were collected through offline over four months (October 2025 to January 2026).  After screening for completeness and inconsistency, a total of 741 valid responses were retained. This sample substantially exceeds the requirement of using PLS-SEM (Hair et al., 2021) and provides robust statistical power, both for path coefficient estimation and moderation testing.

4.4 Bias assessment and analytical strategy
Common method bias (CMB) was assessed through Harman's single-factor test (no dominant single factor) and full collinearity VIF assessment (Kock, 2017). All VIF values were below the 3.3 threshold value confirming manageable CMB levels. PLS-SEM using SmartPLS 4 was selected for four methodological reasons. First, it includes an interaction term (TRE×ITT) which can be handled efficiently through product-indicator approach. Second, the predictive orientation of the study aligns with variance-explanation objective function of PLS-SEM. Lastly, distribution-free estimation is robust to non-normality of Likert-scale and PLS-SEM is most suitable for comparative studies (Hair et al., 2021). Since this study considers three theories of adoption: ToT, TPB, and TRE, PLS-SEM is most suitable for methodological comparison. The two-stage protocol (Anderson and Gerbing, 1988) assessed the measurement adequacy (outer loadings > 0.70, CR > 0.80, AVE>0.50, HTMT<0.85) prior to structural estimation through 5,000 sub-sample bootstrapping.

5.6 Structural model and hypothesis testing
All the structural path results have been presented in Table 6. All eight hypotheses are supported at p ≤ 0.005. ATF exerts the largest absolute path effect on ITT (β = −0.316, t=12.333, p< 0.001, f²=0.227) establishing the loss-aversion asymmetry. This is most theoretically consequential finding of this study. ATF's inhibitory force substantially exceeds ATS's reinforcing contribution (β=0.255) and ATP's process-valence contribution (β=0.197). This is a large effect confirming the capability primacy over attitudinal enthusiasm in forming committed trial intentions. 6. Discussion
6.1 Loss-aversion architecture: Failure aversion dominates success enthusiasm
The most theoretically consequential finding of this study is asymmetric attitudinal pattern where ATF (β = −0.316, f² = 0.227) exerts larger absolute effect on ITT than ATS (β = 0.255, f² = 0.203) and ATP (β = 0.197, f² = 0.074). This asymmetry constitutes empirical confirmation of the core theoretical claim of ToT that failure-anticipatory attitudes operate through an independent avoidance-motivation pathway. Moreover, such attitude is disproportionately powerful relative to equivalent-magnitude approach motivations that provide empirical grounding for loss-aversion principle of prospect’s theory (Kahneman and Tversky, 2013) in AI-enabled educational adoption context. This finding cannot be detected by TAM or UTAUT which are significantly blind to the active inhibitory mechanism. Chaouali et al. (2017) previously documented this asymmetric (ATF > ATS) pattern in financial technology. The study replicates and theoretically extends it to AI-educational services by establishing the cross-domain generalisability of the loss-aversion mechanism in complex technology adoption.
The practical and strategic significance of this finding reorients the dominant EdTech communication approach. Most AI adoption promotion campaigns emphasise performance benefits (activating ATS) and ease of use (activating ATP) but neglects the active inhibitory force of failure aversion. The asymmetry result implies that interventions reducing the perceived cost of unsuccessful experimentation may suggest larger adoption returns than equivalent investments in benefit communication. Structured low-stakes trial environments, transparent algorithmic failure recovery interfaces, and explicit 'no consequence for wrong answers' framing lower ATF without requiring costly product modifications. This insight is derivable only from the ToT's tripartite architecture and requires the theoretical and empirical specification of failure as an independent motivational construct, not a cognitive afterthought.

6.2 Capability primacy: PBC as the highest-leverage positive predictor
PBC emerged as the dominant positive predictor of ITT (β=0.412, f²=0.306) with largest effect that substantially exceeds the contributions of all three attitudinal constructs individually. This finding is theoretically coherent within Bandura's (2014) social cognitive framework. This framework signifies that high self-efficacy fosters exploratory behaviour, persistence through initial difficulty and resilience. Precisely, these characteristics have been identified as components of effective striving by theory of trying. The capability-primacy finding replicates and extends capability barriers as the primary inhibitor of AI adoption in Indonesian higher education (Nurtanto et al., 2025) and PBC primacy finding in Chinese design contexts (Jiao and Cao's (2024). This establishes cross-cultural and cross-domain convergence of various constructs. The mechanism underlying capability dominance within the ToT is specific which signifies that effective striving requires sufficient capability confidence to sustain the effortful engagement and producing outcomes. Without PBC, even strong positive ATS and ATP can’t produce committed ITT as this striving process is non-negotiable. This is fundamentally different from perceived ease of use of TAM and this difference generates distinct intervention requirements.

6.3 Normative social embeddedness in collectivist educational culture
SNO exerted a significant positive effect on ITT (β = 0.262, f² = 0.218) and confirms that normative-social mechanism (through instructor endorsement, peer modelling, and institutional legitimation) shapes AI trial intentions. This finding converges with Feng et al. (2025) and Shneor et al. (2021) and also consistent with the TPB's established evidence base (Ajzen, 1991; Teo, 2011) and social influence of UTAUT (Venkatesh et al., 2003). This study incorporates SNO through the TPB to preserve theoretical alignment with the ToT. In India's collectivist educational context where hierarchical authority relationships and peer cohort identity, the amplified SNO effect carry heightened motivational salience. This confirms the cultural sensitivity of normative adoption mechanism. The implication is that AI adoption in Indian higher education is not solely an individual motivational challenge, but a social-normative. The same AI tool deployed in a normatively endorsing environment will achieve higher ITT rates than ambiguous environment which is independent of tool utility and ease of use. 

6.4 The sophistication effect: Technological readiness as double-edged capability
TRE's counterintuitive negative direct effect on ABE (β = -0.112), when ITT is controlled, constitutes the most practical and provocative finding of this study. It challenges the assumption that readiness promotes adoption amongst learners who are dynamic and technically capable but lack strong trial intentions. Such learners withhold sustained adoption pending satisfactory evidences that bring value to them. Dwivedi et al. (2021) have documented this pattern in AI literacy critique amplification and Han et al. (2024) provide converging evidence that higher technology capability produces more stringent use and evaluation criteria. Within the theory of trying, this corresponds to technically ready learners attempting AI tools and evaluating themselves against high standards. Such learners find adoption-threshold performance unmet and withhold sustained use. This is mirror image of the adoption pathway provided by the theory of trying. The positive moderating effect of TRE (β = 0.086) provides the resolution that when strong ITT and high TRE occur simultaneously, the evaluation threshold is met and motivational conversion is amplified rather than suppressed. Combining committed trial motivation of learners with dispositional digital competence encounters fewer barriers and demonstrates greater optimism through initial difficulties. As a result, learners leverage their technological sophistication to translate motivated commitment into sustained use (Ayanwale and Ndlovu, 2024).

6.5 Tri-theoretic integration: Superior explanatory architecture
Collectively, the structural results demonstrate that the tri-theoretic (ToT-TPB-TRT) integration achieves explanatory coverage (R² = 0.697 for ITT), substantially higher than TAM and UTAUT based models, in comparable AI adoption contexts (Nurtanto et al., 2025; Sharma et al., 2025). More theoretically, the framework specifies and tests the independent motivational contribution of failure attitudes, normative forces, capability beliefs, and dispositional readiness, a comprehensive architecture that positive-valence frameworks are unable to achieve. Thereby, the study advances a precise theoretical claim that TAM and UTAUT are not merely incomplete accounts of AI adoption motivation rather they are misspecified accounts that attribute adoption failures to insufficient positive affect. Such failures are substantially driven by active inhibitory forces and capability constraints requiring distinct theoretical constructs and different interventions.

7. Theoretical and managerial contributions
7.1 Theoretical contributions
First: Validation of theory of trying in AI-enabled educational services. This study provides the first rigorous empirical application and validation of the theory of trying in AI-educational adoption. This extends the empirical contributions of the ToT from consumer behaviour and mobile commerce (Chaouali et al., 2017; Issock et al., 2021) to the domain where its theoretical architecture is most suited. It is established that all three attitudinal constructs of ToT are significant predictors (with relative magnitude ATF>ATS>ATP) of loss-aversion. This validates the theory of trying is theoretically superior for complex and uncertain technology adoption. This outcome couldn’t be achieved by prior studies based on TAM and UTAUT models. 
Second: Loss-aversion asymmetry as a structurally necessary AI adoption mechanism. The empirical confirmation (β_ATF=0.316 > β_ATS=0.255) establishes the loss-aversion asymmetry as an empirical regularity in AI-educational adoption. This extends the prospect theory (Kahneman and Tversky's, 2013) for financial decision making to technological adoption in educational services. This constitutes a framework-level finding that failure attitudes (independent of success attitudes) are misspecified for the adoption challenges. Such findings are theorised by previous frameworks. 
Third: TRT as ToT boundary condition and moderation extension. The confirmation provided by the study that moderation of ITT-to-ABE and conversion by TRE (β = 0.086, p = 0.005) advances TRT by specifying its boundary condition function within the framework of the theory of trying. Such role hasn’t been previously theorised in the literature related to technology readiness (TRE). This finding establishes that neither motivation (ITT) nor readiness (TRE) alone determines adoption, rather interaction of both does. This advances both, TRT as a new moderating context and ToT as the dispositional enabling condition for conversion of intention into adoption. 
Fourth: The sophistication effect as a theoretically grounded adoption paradox. The negative direct TRE → ABE path (β = −0.112) with controlled ITT constitutes a novel and priori theorised empirical finding with direct theoretical implications. Technically capable learners are not simply high-probability adopters, but they are critical evaluators seeking stringent quality standards before adoption. This finding challenges the implicit universal pro-adoption assumption regarding capability variables and establishes the qualifying condition under which readiness promotes or suppresses adoption.
Fifth: Tri-theoretic integration demonstrating superiority over single-framework alternatives. The R² = 0.697 for ITT and coherent five-construct antecedent pattern demonstrates that ToT-TPB-TRT integration achieves explanatory coverage which no single prior framework provides. The study demonstrates that ToT, TPB, and TRT are theoretically complementary frameworks where ToT provides attitudinal and motivational architecture, TPB supplies normative and capability dimensions and TRT contributes the dispositional enabling boundary condition.

7.2 Managerial contributions
Prioritise failure cost reduction over benefit amplification. The ATF dominance finding implies that developers of AI based education should redesign adoption promotion around reducing anxiety of failure anxiety, rather than promoting performance benefits. Practically, sandbox trial environments with no academic consequences, transparent algorithmic error correction interfaces, progressive disclosure features that scaffold learners through AI complexity, and safe experimentation should be considered while designing the courses. Institutions continuing to emphasise on utility (without addressing failure anxiety) are targeting the wrong learners. This study quantifies this error ATF has 24% larger absolute effect over ATS.
Capability building as the highest-leverage institutional investment. PBC's large-effect dominance (f² = 0.306) signals that structured capability development determines trial intentions. Embedding AI in digital literacy programmes (covering technical operation) is associated with evaluation of algorithmic outputs. Data privacy management should be integrated into curricula as learning requirements, not supplementary workshops. Peer mentoring schemes by pairing technically confident students with less capable peers, interventions targeting PBC and could plausibly improve SNO effects. Technical infrastructure provisioning (device access, reliable internet, AI tool licensing) addresses PBC's external facilitating conditions.
Leverage institutional normative levers. SNO's significant effect (f² = 0.218) identifies instructor behaviour and institutional policy as strategically controllable normative levers. Instructors, who visibly integrate AI tools into course delivery, send injunctive normative signals and legitimise AI experimentation. An institutional AI adoption policy establishing clear expectations and publicly celebrating AI-enhanced learning outcomes create normative environments and reduce adoption barriers. For policymakers, regulatory frameworks for AI in education should explicitly address normative environment design as an adoption enabler.
Differentiated product strategy for high vs. low TRE learner segments. The sophistication effect implies that technical developers can’t apply uniform adoption promotion across learners. For high-TRE learners, adoption depends on satisfying stringent evidence standards: transparent learning efficacy data, independent pedagogical effectiveness certifications, and clear privacy disclosures. For low-TRE learners, motivational interventions require parallel optimism and digital confidence development. Facilitating dimensions of TRE must be built before or alongside motivational interventions, not after. This segmentation represents a departure from the dominant features-benefit promotion model towards evidence-based promotion model.

8. Conclusion, limitations, and future research
8.1 Conclusion
The persistent gap between learners' intention and their sustained adoption of AI-enabled educational services demand theoretically rigorous modelling that captures the motivational complexity of uncertain, effortful, and failure-susceptible goal pursuit. This study demonstrates that the ToT, enriched by the TPB's normative and capability constructs and anchored by TRE as a dispositional moderating boundary condition provides a theoretically superior and empirically powerful account. This signifies why some learners convert trial motivation into adoption and others don’t. The central theoretical advancement is specific and portable which portrays that failure attitudes are not the absence of success enthusiasm rather they constitute an active inhibitory force operating through a loss-aversion mechanism. This can’t be captured by positive-valence frameworks structurally which generates materially different intervention requirements.
The sophistication effect (high TRE suppressing direct adoption while amplifying motivationally-grounded adoption through interaction with ITT) constitutes the most theoretically provocative and practically actionable finding of this study. It establishes that technology adoption strategy is unable to treat capability as uniformly pro-adoption as technically capable but less-motivated learners are critical evaluators requiring evidence-based performance. The tri-theoretic integration (ToT+TPB+TRT) demonstrates that theoretical adequacy requires addressing the inhibitory forces, capability prerequisites, normative conditions, and dispositional enabling boundary conditions of the adoption challenge simultaneously. TAM and UTAUT models are unable to provide this complete motivational architecture.
As AI systems permeate consequential domains beyond education (healthcare, legal services, financial advice, administration), the adoption challenges will increasingly resemble the pattern documented here. These may be high stated intentions, modest actual conversion, and adoption failure driven by active inhibitory forces and capability constraints rather than insufficient positive affect. Frameworks that capture these dynamics will be progressively more necessary for adoption of AI-enabled contexts.

8.2 Limitations
Four honest limitations qualify for this study. First, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference and cannot observe the temporal dynamics of the sequential striving process of the theory of trying from initial motivation formation to adoption. Second, reliance on self-reported measures for all constructs (including ABE) introduces common method variance, social desirability bias, and retrospective recall bias that Harman's test and VIF diagnostics could mitigate but can’t eliminate. Third, geographic confinement to Delhi-NCR limits generalisability to other geographical locations in India with different AI infrastructure, cultural orientations, and institutional environments. Fourth, the TRE operationalisation using only optimism and innovativeness sub-scales produces a theoretically incomplete TRE specification that is likely to attenuate the TRE-ABE direct effect estimate and may conflate facilitating and inhibiting TRE dynamics.

8.3 Future research directions
Five specific future directions emerge from this study. First, longitudinal experience-sampling designs should track the same learners through initial exposure. Additionally, iterative trials, early adoption, sustained use, and enabling observation of the sequential striving process of the theory of trying may help in establishing causal direction in the motivation-adoption relationship. Second, cross-cultural replications should extend the tri-theoretic framework to Western higher education contexts where individualist cultural orientations may attenuate SNO effects relative to the collectivist amplification. It may help in testing the cultural boundary conditions of the normative adoption mechanism. Third, trust in AI should be incorporated as a theorised mediator of the ATF-ITT relationship. Algorithmic opacity and surveillance concerns generate trust-mediated failure anxiety, and this mediation channel deserves explicit specification and testing. Fourth, full operationalisation of TRI 2.0 (including discomfort and insecurity dimensions) would enable to completed theoretical specification of TRE. It may potentially reveal that inhibiting dimensions drive the sophistication effect while facilitating dimensions drive the moderation amplification. Last, integration of objective LMS usage data alongside self-reports would provide construct validity for ABE independent of common method variance and shall enable precise ITT-ABE conversion rate estimation at the individual level.
